## Understanding Depression Severity

Depression isn't one thing. How severe yours is shapes which treatments make sense and what insurance will cover.

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) is the standard screening tool. Psychiatrists use it. Primary care doctors use it. TMS clinics use it. And insurance companies look at your score when deciding whether to authorize TMS.

## The PHQ-9 Questionnaire

Over the **last 2 weeks**, how often have you been bothered by each of the following?

Score each item: **0** = Not at all, **1** = Several days, **2** = More than half the days, **3** = Nearly every day

1. Little interest or pleasure in doing things
2. Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless
3. Trouble falling or staying asleep, or sleeping too much
4. Feeling tired or having little energy
5. Poor appetite or overeating
6. Feeling bad about yourself — or that you are a failure or have let yourself or your family down
7. Trouble concentrating on things, such as reading the newspaper or watching television
8. Moving or speaking so slowly that other people could have noticed — or the opposite — being so fidgety or restless that you have been moving around a lot more than usual
9. Thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself in some way

**Add your scores for all 9 items.**

## What Your Score Means

| PHQ-9 Score | Severity | Typical Treatment Approach |
|---|---|---|
| 0–4 | Minimal or none | Monitoring, lifestyle changes |
| 5–9 | Mild | Watchful waiting, therapy, possible medication |
| 10–14 | Moderate | Therapy and/or medication |
| 15–19 | Moderately severe | Medication and therapy; TMS if medications fail |
| 20–27 | Severe | Aggressive treatment; strong TMS candidate if treatment-resistant |

## Where TMS Fits In

TMS is typically recommended when you score **10 or above** on the PHQ-9 and antidepressants haven't done enough. Most insurance companies want to see:

- A PHQ-9 score of 10+ at the time of your TMS evaluation
- Documentation of at least 2 failed medication trials
- Depression that persists despite current treatment

### What "Treatment-Resistant" Means

You don't need to have tried everything. Treatment-resistant depression generally means:

- **Minimum:** 2 medication trials at the right dose for 6+ weeks each that didn't work well enough
- **Typical insurance requirement:** 2–4 failed trials, with documentation from your prescriber
- **What counts as "failed":** Less than 50% improvement in symptoms, or side effects bad enough to prevent adequate dosing

## Important Notes

- This is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. Only a licensed clinician can diagnose depression.
- **If you scored 1 or higher on question 9** (thoughts of self-harm), please reach out to a mental health professional or call the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988).
- Your PHQ-9 score fluctuates. One high score doesn't mean you need immediate intervention. One low score doesn't mean you're fine if you've been struggling for months.

## Track Your Score Over Time

TMS clinics use the PHQ-9 before, during, and after treatment to track progress. Here's what a successful TMS course typically looks like:

- **Baseline (pre-treatment):** PHQ-9 of 15–22
- **Mid-treatment (week 3):** PHQ-9 drops to 10–15
- **End of treatment (week 6):** PHQ-9 of 5–10
- **Response:** 50% or greater reduction from baseline
- **Remission:** PHQ-9 below 5

About 50–60% of TMS patients achieve response, and 30–35% reach full remission.
