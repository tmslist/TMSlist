## Active stimulation vs brain training

TMS directly stimulates brain circuits with magnetic pulses. Neurofeedback teaches the brain to self-regulate by providing real-time feedback on brainwave patterns. Both are non-invasive, but they differ significantly in evidence quality, mechanism, and insurance coverage.

### How they work

**TMS:** Magnetic coil delivers focused pulses to specific brain regions (typically left DLPFC). The stimulation directly changes neural activity patterns. No effort required from the patient.

**Neurofeedback:** EEG sensors monitor brainwaves while the patient watches a screen or plays a game. When brainwave patterns move toward desired states, positive feedback is given (screen brightens, game progresses). Over time, the brain learns to maintain healthier patterns.

### Evidence comparison

| Factor | TMS | Neurofeedback |
|--------|-----|--------------|
| FDA status for depression | Approved (2008) | Not approved |
| Randomized controlled trials | 100+ | Limited; many lack sham controls |
| Meta-analyses | Multiple, favorable | Few; mixed conclusions |
| Insurance coverage | Yes (after med failures) | Rarely covered |
| APA/guidelines recommendation | Yes | Not currently recommended for depression |

The evidence gap is significant. TMS has robust Level 1 evidence (large RCTs, meta-analyses, FDA approval). Neurofeedback evidence is weaker — many studies lack proper sham controls, and it's unclear whether benefits exceed placebo.

### Cost

- **TMS:** $6,000-$12,000 for full course. Insurance covers for depression.
- **Neurofeedback:** $100-$200 per session, typically 30-60 sessions needed ($3,000-$12,000). Rarely covered by insurance.

### Session experience

**TMS:** 19-37 minutes, sit in chair, tapping sensation. 36 sessions over 6-9 weeks.

**Neurofeedback:** 30-60 minutes, watch screen with EEG cap on. 30-60 sessions over 3-6 months. Requires sustained attention and engagement.

### Who should consider which

**TMS if:**
- You want FDA-approved, evidence-based treatment
- Insurance coverage is important
- You prefer a shorter treatment course
- Depression is your primary concern

**Neurofeedback may be considered if:**
- You've exhausted standard options including TMS
- ADHD is a primary concern (neurofeedback has more evidence for ADHD than depression)
- You want a drug-free, non-stimulation approach
- You're willing to invest significant time and self-pay

### The bottom line

For depression, TMS has substantially stronger evidence and should be tried first. Neurofeedback is an interesting technology but lacks the rigorous evidence base needed to recommend it as a depression treatment. For ADHD, neurofeedback has somewhat better support but remains controversial.
