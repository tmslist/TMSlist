## How TMS and ECT compare

Both TMS and ECT treat depression by stimulating the brain, but they work very differently. ECT uses electrical currents under general anesthesia to induce brief seizures. TMS uses focused magnetic pulses on specific brain regions with no anesthesia required.

### Efficacy

- **ECT response rate:** 70-90% (the most effective treatment for severe, treatment-resistant depression)
- **TMS response rate:** 50-60% (strong for moderate treatment-resistant cases)
- **ECT remission rate:** 50-60%
- **TMS remission rate:** 30-35%

ECT is generally more effective for severe cases, including depression with psychotic features or acute suicidality. TMS is better suited for patients who have failed 1-3 medication trials but don't require the intensity of ECT.

### Side effects

**TMS side effects** are mild and localized:
- Scalp discomfort at the treatment site (most common, fades over sessions)
- Mild headache (resolves within hours)
- No cognitive side effects
- No anesthesia required

**ECT side effects** are more significant:
- Short-term memory loss (most common concern)
- Confusion immediately after treatment
- Muscle aches, nausea, headache
- Requires general anesthesia for each session
- Cognitive effects typically resolve within weeks, but some patients report longer-lasting memory issues

### Treatment schedule

| Factor | TMS | ECT |
|--------|-----|-----|
| Sessions | 36 sessions over 6-9 weeks | 6-12 sessions over 3-4 weeks |
| Session length | 19-37 minutes | ~1 hour (including recovery) |
| Anesthesia | None | General anesthesia each session |
| Drive after? | Yes | No (need someone to drive you) |
| Work impact | Minimal — most patients continue working | Significant — recovery time needed |

### Cost

- **TMS:** $6,000-$12,000 for full course (often covered by insurance after medication failures)
- **ECT:** $10,000-$25,000+ for full course (usually covered when medically necessary)

### Who should choose which?

**Consider TMS if:**
- You've failed 1-3 antidepressants
- You want to avoid anesthesia and cognitive side effects
- You need to continue working during treatment
- Your depression is moderate to severe

**Consider ECT if:**
- You've failed multiple treatments including TMS
- Your depression is severe with psychotic features
- You need rapid response (acute suicidality)
- You're willing to accept temporary cognitive side effects for higher efficacy

### The bottom line

TMS and ECT aren't competing treatments — they serve different points on the severity spectrum. Many patients try TMS first because of its milder side-effect profile, then consider ECT if TMS doesn't achieve adequate response. Talk to your psychiatrist about which approach matches your clinical situation.
