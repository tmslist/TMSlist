## 3-minute sessions vs 37-minute sessions

Theta burst stimulation (TBS) is a newer TMS protocol that delivers treatment in a fraction of the time. Standard rTMS takes 37 minutes per session. Intermittent TBS (iTBS) takes just 3 minutes — delivering the same number of pulses in a condensed burst pattern.

### How they differ

**Standard rTMS:**
- 10Hz repetitive pulses
- 3,000 pulses per session
- 37 minutes per session
- 36 sessions over 6-9 weeks
- The original FDA-cleared protocol (2008)

**Intermittent Theta Burst (iTBS):**
- Bursts of 3 pulses at 50Hz, repeated at 5Hz intervals
- 600 pulses per session (fewer but more potent bursts)
- 3 minutes per session
- 36 sessions over 6-9 weeks
- FDA-cleared (NeuroStar Express) based on the THREE-D trial

### The THREE-D trial

The landmark 2018 THREE-D trial (published in The Lancet) randomized 414 patients to iTBS vs standard 10Hz rTMS. Key findings:

- **iTBS was non-inferior to standard rTMS** — equivalent efficacy
- Response rate: iTBS 49% vs standard 47% (no significant difference)
- Remission rate: iTBS 32% vs standard 28% (no significant difference)
- iTBS took 3 minutes vs 37 minutes
- No significant difference in side effects

This trial established iTBS as an equivalent, faster alternative.

### Advantages of theta burst

- **Time:** 3 minutes vs 37 minutes — transformative for patient convenience
- **Clinic throughput:** Clinics can treat more patients per day
- **Patient compliance:** Easier to commit to 3-minute appointments
- **Cost potential:** Shorter sessions could reduce costs (though most clinics charge the same)

### When standard rTMS may be preferred

- Some patients report less scalp discomfort with the longer, more gradual standard protocol
- Patients who haven't responded to iTBS may try standard rTMS (different stimulation pattern)
- Some clinics only have standard rTMS capability (not all devices support theta burst)
- Certain research protocols still use standard parameters

### Comfort comparison

**Standard rTMS:** Rhythmic tapping sensation, consistent intensity throughout 37 minutes. Patients habituate to the sensation over the session.

**iTBS:** Intense bursts with pauses between them. The bursts feel sharper but the total exposure is 3 minutes. Some patients find the burst pattern more intense; others prefer getting it over quickly.

### Cost

Most clinics charge the same per session regardless of protocol:
- $200-$400 per session for either
- Insurance covers both equally
- The cost difference, if any, is minimal

### Which should you choose?

**Choose iTBS (theta burst) if:**
- Time is a factor (busy schedule, long commute)
- You want the most convenient option
- Your clinic offers it (not all do)

**Choose standard rTMS if:**
- You're more comfortable with gradual stimulation
- iTBS hasn't worked and you want to try a different pattern
- Your clinic only offers standard protocols

**The bottom line:** iTBS and standard rTMS produce equivalent outcomes. The choice is primarily about convenience and preference, not efficacy.
