## TMS for young people

Adolescent depression is a growing crisis — rates have doubled since 2020. While SSRIs and therapy are first-line treatments, many teens don't respond adequately. TMS is emerging as a safe alternative, though it's not yet FDA-approved for patients under 18.

## FDA status and evidence

- **FDA clearance:** TMS is cleared for adults 18+ with treatment-resistant depression. Adolescent use is off-label.
- **Growing evidence:** Over 20 studies have examined TMS in adolescents (ages 12-17)
- **Safety:** No serious adverse events reported in adolescent TMS studies
- **Efficacy:** Response rates of 55-75% in adolescent studies (potentially higher than in adults)
- **Guidelines:** The American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry recognizes TMS as a potential option for treatment-resistant adolescent depression

## Safety considerations for teens

- **Brain development:** The adolescent brain is still developing. Current evidence shows no adverse effects on neurodevelopment, but long-term data is limited
- **Seizure threshold:** Slightly lower in adolescents; protocols may use conservative parameters
- **Consent:** Requires both parental consent and adolescent assent
- **Monitoring:** More frequent mood monitoring recommended (depression can fluctuate more rapidly in teens)

## Practical considerations

**School scheduling:**
- 36 sessions over 6-9 weeks means daily absences
- Theta burst (3-minute sessions) minimizes time away from school
- Some families schedule sessions before or after school
- Consider starting during summer or school breaks

**Family involvement:**
- Parents typically transport teen to daily sessions
- Family therapy alongside TMS may enhance outcomes
- Educate the whole family about what to expect

**Insurance:**
- Coverage for adolescent TMS varies — some insurers cover off-label use
- Document medication failures and therapy attempts thoroughly
- Consider peer-to-peer review if initially denied

## When to consider TMS for your teen

- Failed 2+ antidepressant trials (including at least one SSRI)
- Therapy (CBT) hasn't achieved adequate response
- Depression is significantly impairing school, social life, or safety
- Family prefers non-medication approach
- Side effects of antidepressants are problematic (weight gain, emotional blunting, suicidal ideation concern)
