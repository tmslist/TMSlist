## TMS for young people

Teen depression has doubled since 2020. That's not a talking point — it's a crisis. SSRIs and therapy are the standard first-line treatments, but plenty of teens don't get enough relief from those. TMS is emerging as a safe alternative, though it's not yet FDA-approved for anyone under 18.

## FDA status and evidence

- **FDA clearance:** TMS is cleared for adults 18+ with treatment-resistant depression. For adolescents, it's off-label.
- **Growing evidence:** Over 20 studies have looked at TMS in adolescents (ages 12-17)
- **Safety:** No serious adverse events reported in adolescent TMS studies
- **Efficacy:** Response rates of 55-75% in adolescent studies — potentially higher than in adults
- **Professional recognition:** The American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry recognizes TMS as a potential option for treatment-resistant adolescent depression

## Safety considerations for teens

- **Brain development:** The adolescent brain is still maturing. Current evidence shows no adverse effects on neurodevelopment, though long-term data is limited
- **Seizure threshold:** Slightly lower in adolescents, so protocols may use more conservative parameters
- **Consent:** Both parental consent and the teen's assent are required
- **Monitoring:** More frequent mood checks are recommended — depression can shift faster in teens

## Practical considerations

**School scheduling:**
Daily sessions for 6-9 weeks means absences. There's no way around it.
- Theta burst (3-minute sessions) minimizes the disruption
- Some families schedule sessions before or after school hours
- Starting during summer or a school break is worth considering

**Family involvement:**
- A parent typically drives the teen to daily sessions
- Family therapy alongside TMS may boost outcomes
- Educate the whole family on what to expect — this is a team effort

**Insurance:**
- Coverage for adolescent TMS varies — some insurers will cover off-label use
- Thorough documentation of medication failures and therapy attempts helps
- If initially denied, request a peer-to-peer review

## When to consider TMS for your teen

- They've tried 2+ antidepressants (including at least one SSRI) without enough improvement
- Therapy (CBT) hasn't gotten them where they need to be
- Depression is seriously affecting school, friendships, or safety
- Your family prefers a non-medication approach
- Antidepressant side effects are a problem — weight gain, emotional blunting, or the suicidal ideation concern that keeps parents up at night
