## First responders and mental health

Police officers, firefighters, paramedics, and EMTs face repeated trauma exposure, high-stress environments, and a culture that often stigmatizes mental health treatment. Depression and PTSD rates among first responders are 2-5x higher than the general population, yet fewer than 35% seek treatment.

## Why TMS works for first responders

- **No medication side effects:** No drowsiness, slowed reflexes, or cognitive impairment that could affect job performance
- **No substance concerns:** Unlike benzodiazepines, TMS has no abuse potential and won't affect fitness-for-duty evaluations
- **Confidential:** Treatment doesn't appear on pharmacy records or drug screenings
- **Compatible with shift work:** Flexible scheduling accommodates irregular hours
- **Quick sessions:** Theta burst (3 minutes) fits between shifts

## Coverage options

- **Employee health insurance:** Most first responder health plans cover TMS for depression
- **Workers' compensation:** If depression is related to occupational trauma/injury
- **IAFF/IAFC programs:** Firefighter unions increasingly cover behavioral health including TMS
- **Fraternal Order of Police:** Some local lodges have mental health benefits
- **First responder charities:** Organizations like Code Green Campaign and First Responders Children's Foundation may assist with costs

## Addressing the stigma barrier

TMS may be more acceptable to first responders than traditional mental health treatment:
- It's a medical procedure, not "therapy" — may feel less stigmatizing
- No ongoing medication to manage or disclose
- Quick sessions don't require long absences from duty
- Results are measurable (PHQ-9 scores) — appeals to data-oriented professionals

## Practical scheduling

- **Before shift:** Many TMS clinics open at 7am
- **After shift:** Evening appointments available at some clinics
- **Kelly day/days off:** Schedule sessions on regular days off
- **Theta burst:** 3-minute sessions allow treatment during any break
- **Accelerated protocols:** Complete treatment in 2-3 weeks for those on extended leave

## What to tell your department

You are not required to disclose TMS treatment. However, if seeking time off:
- TMS is a medical procedure — qualifies for sick leave and FMLA
- Peer support programs can help navigate department processes
- Many departments are becoming more supportive of proactive mental health care
