## TMS for cocaine use disorder

Cocaine addiction involves impaired prefrontal cortex function — the brain region responsible for impulse control and decision-making. When this area is weakened, the reward circuit's drive to seek cocaine overwhelms the ability to resist. TMS targets this imbalance by strengthening prefrontal activity.

TMS is **not FDA-approved** for cocaine addiction or any substance use disorder, but cocaine is one of the most-studied addiction targets for TMS.

## Key research findings

- A landmark 2016 Italian study found bilateral DLPFC rTMS reduced cocaine use by 69% compared to sham
- A 2022 meta-analysis of 9 RCTs confirmed significant reductions in cocaine craving and use
- Deep TMS (targeting insula and medial PFC) may be more effective than standard rTMS for substance cravings
- Effects on craving are typically rapid (within first week); effects on actual use take 2-4 weeks

## Why cocaine addiction is particularly suited for TMS

Unlike alcohol or opioids, there are **no FDA-approved medications** for cocaine use disorder. This makes non-pharmacological approaches like TMS especially valuable:
- No drug interactions to manage
- Can be combined with behavioral therapy
- Targets the neurological basis of impaired control

## Treatment protocols

- **Standard protocol:** 10-15Hz to left DLPFC, 10-20 sessions over 2-4 weeks
- **Deep TMS:** H-coil bilateral PFC + insula, 15-20 sessions
- **Maintenance:** Weekly or biweekly sessions may be needed to sustain benefit
- **Combined approach:** TMS + contingency management or CBT shows enhanced outcomes in preliminary studies

## Important considerations

- Not a standalone treatment — must be part of comprehensive addiction care
- Active intoxication or severe withdrawal should be managed before TMS
- Seizure risk assessment needed (stimulant use can lower seizure threshold)
- Insurance does not cover for this indication
- Look for clinical trials at addiction research centers
