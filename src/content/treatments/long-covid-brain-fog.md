## Long COVID and the brain

An estimated 10-30% of COVID-19 survivors experience persistent cognitive symptoms — commonly called "brain fog." This includes difficulty concentrating, memory problems, slowed processing speed, and mental fatigue that can persist months to years after infection.

Neuroimaging studies show that long COVID affects the prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, and white matter connectivity — the same circuits involved in depression and that TMS can modulate.

## Why TMS is being investigated

TMS may address long COVID brain fog through several mechanisms:

- **Neuroplasticity:** Repetitive stimulation promotes new neural connections in areas damaged by neuroinflammation
- **Prefrontal activation:** Stimulating the DLPFC can improve executive function, working memory, and attention
- **Anti-inflammatory effects:** Some evidence suggests rTMS has neuroinflammatory modulating properties
- **Depression comorbidity:** Many long COVID patients have concurrent depression, which TMS is proven to treat

## What the research shows

TMS for long COVID is a new research area, but early studies are encouraging:

- A 2024 pilot study at Yale found 10 sessions of DLPFC rTMS improved cognitive performance scores by 25-40% in long COVID patients
- A Brazilian RCT (2023) showed significant improvement in attention and processing speed after 20 sessions of high-frequency rTMS
- Case series from multiple centers report improvements in "brain fog" severity ratings and return-to-work capability
- Deep TMS targeting broader prefrontal networks is under investigation

## Treatment protocols being studied

- **Standard DLPFC rTMS:** 10Hz, left DLPFC, 3000 pulses/session, 20-30 sessions
- **Theta burst stimulation:** Accelerated protocols (3-minute sessions) showing promise
- **Bilateral stimulation:** Some protocols target both left and right DLPFC
- **Combined approaches:** TMS + cognitive rehabilitation exercises

## Current limitations

- **Not FDA-approved** for long COVID or cognitive symptoms
- **Insurance:** Generally not covered for this indication
- **Variable definitions:** "Brain fog" isn't a standardized diagnosis, making research challenging
- **Small studies:** Most evidence comes from pilot studies with 20-50 participants
- **Unknown optimal protocol:** The best stimulation parameters for cognitive vs. mood symptoms may differ

## Who might benefit

Based on current evidence, TMS for long COVID may be most helpful for patients who:

- Have documented cognitive deficits on neuropsychological testing
- Are 3+ months post-acute infection with persistent symptoms
- Have not improved with standard rehabilitation approaches
- Also have depression symptoms (which TMS can treat simultaneously)

## Finding treatment

- **Clinical trials:** Search ClinicalTrials.gov for "TMS long COVID" or "TMS post-COVID cognitive"
- **Academic medical centers:** Long COVID clinics at major hospitals may offer TMS as part of research protocols
- **Off-label treatment:** Some TMS clinics offer treatment for cognitive symptoms; confirm the provider understands long COVID neurology
- **Cost:** Expect to self-pay ($200-$400 per session) as insurance doesn't cover this indication
