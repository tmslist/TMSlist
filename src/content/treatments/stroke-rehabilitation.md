## TMS in stroke recovery

After a stroke, the damaged hemisphere becomes underactive while the unaffected hemisphere often becomes overactive — creating an imbalance that hinders recovery. TMS can address this imbalance by either stimulating the damaged side or calming the overactive side.

TMS for stroke rehabilitation is **not FDA-approved** but is supported by a growing body of evidence, particularly for motor recovery and post-stroke aphasia.

## Motor recovery

**The interhemispheric imbalance model:**
After a stroke affecting one hemisphere, the intact hemisphere exerts excessive inhibition on the damaged side, further suppressing recovery.

**TMS approaches:**
1. **Excitatory rTMS (10-20Hz) to the affected hemisphere** — boost activity in the damaged motor cortex
2. **Inhibitory rTMS (1Hz) to the unaffected hemisphere** — reduce excessive cross-inhibition
3. **Combined approach** — both in sequence

**Evidence:**
- A 2022 Cochrane review of 38 studies found rTMS improved upper limb motor function when combined with physical therapy
- Improvements in grip strength, dexterity, and functional use of the affected hand
- Effects are larger when TMS is delivered during the subacute phase (1-6 months post-stroke) but can help in chronic stroke too
- Best results when paired with intensive rehabilitation exercises

## Post-stroke aphasia

For patients who lose language ability after a left-hemisphere stroke:

- Low-frequency (1Hz) rTMS to the right hemisphere Broca's area homologue reduces maladaptive compensation
- Studies show improvement in naming ability, fluency, and comprehension
- Effects are enhanced when combined with speech therapy
- A 2023 meta-analysis of 19 studies confirmed significant language improvement

## Post-stroke depression

30-40% of stroke survivors develop depression, which impairs rehabilitation outcomes:

- Standard depression TMS protocols (left DLPFC, 10Hz) are effective
- May be the most immediately practical TMS application post-stroke
- Treating depression improves engagement with physical and occupational therapy

## Treatment timing

| Phase | Timing | TMS Evidence | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------------|-------|
| Acute | 0-2 weeks | Limited | Focus on medical stabilization |
| Subacute | 2 weeks - 6 months | Strongest | Optimal neuroplastic window |
| Chronic | 6+ months | Moderate | Still beneficial, especially for aphasia |

## Safety considerations

- **Hemorrhagic stroke:** TMS generally avoided in early period after hemorrhagic stroke
- **Seizure risk:** Slightly elevated in post-stroke patients; carefully managed with protocol adjustments
- **Metal implants:** Surgical clips or cranioplasty plates may be contraindications
- **Assessment:** Thorough neurological evaluation before starting TMS

## Practical recommendations

- Discuss TMS with your neurologist and rehabilitation team
- Best used as adjunct to physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy — not a replacement
- Look for rehabilitation hospitals or academic stroke centers with TMS programs
- Clinical trials offer access to structured TMS rehabilitation protocols
- Timing matters — earlier intervention (within 6 months) may yield better results
