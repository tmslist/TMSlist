## TMS and Parkinson's disease

Parkinson's disease (PD) involves progressive loss of dopamine-producing neurons, leading to motor symptoms (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia) and non-motor symptoms (depression, cognitive decline, apathy). TMS is being investigated for both categories of symptoms.

TMS is **not FDA-approved** for Parkinson's disease, but research spans over two decades with hundreds of published studies.

## Motor symptoms

**What's being targeted:**
- Primary motor cortex (M1) — to improve movement initiation and reduce bradykinesia
- Supplementary motor area (SMA) — to help with movement planning
- Cerebellum — to modulate motor circuits

**Evidence:**
- A 2022 Cochrane review found high-frequency rTMS to M1 produced small but statistically significant improvements in motor function (UPDRS motor scores)
- Effects are modest — typically 10-20% improvement in motor scores
- Benefits are short-lived without maintenance (lasting 2-4 weeks after treatment course)
- Deep TMS may produce more durable effects by reaching deeper motor circuits

## Depression in Parkinson's

Depression affects 40-50% of PD patients and significantly impacts quality of life. This is the most promising TMS application in PD:

- Standard left DLPFC rTMS protocols (same as for primary depression) show efficacy in PD-related depression
- Response rates comparable to TMS for non-PD depression (50-60%)
- Advantage over antidepressants: no drug interactions with PD medications (levodopa, dopamine agonists)
- Some patients show simultaneous improvement in both mood and motor symptoms

## Cognitive symptoms

- Early-stage research on DLPFC stimulation for PD cognitive decline
- Some studies show improvement in executive function and processing speed
- Effects on memory are less consistent
- May be most beneficial for PD patients with mild cognitive impairment rather than dementia

## Treatment considerations for PD patients

**Safety:**
- TMS is generally safe in PD patients
- Deep brain stimulation (DBS) implants are a contraindication — the magnetic field can interfere with the device
- Medication timing matters — some protocols schedule TMS during "on" periods for best results

**Practical challenges:**
- Tremor can make head positioning difficult; some clinics use specialized head rests
- Patients with severe rigidity may find the chair uncomfortable
- Cognitive impairment may affect ability to tolerate or cooperate with treatment

## Current recommendations

- TMS for PD depression has the strongest evidence and may be a reasonable option when discussed with your neurologist
- TMS for motor symptoms should be considered experimental — improvements are real but modest
- Always coordinate with your movement disorder specialist before starting TMS
- Do not modify PD medications based on TMS response without neurologist guidance

## Where to find treatment

- **For depression:** Many standard TMS clinics can treat depression in PD patients (same protocol)
- **For motor symptoms:** Look for research protocols at academic movement disorder centers
- **Clinical trials:** Search ClinicalTrials.gov for "TMS Parkinson" — multiple active studies
