## MS fatigue and the brain

Fatigue is the most common and disabling symptom of multiple sclerosis, affecting 75-90% of patients. Unlike ordinary tiredness, MS fatigue involves central nervous system dysfunction — demyelination disrupts the neural pathways that sustain energy, attention, and motor output.

Current treatments for MS fatigue (amantadine, modafinil) have limited efficacy, creating interest in neuromodulation approaches like TMS.

## How TMS may help

TMS addresses MS fatigue through several potential mechanisms:

- **Motor cortex stimulation:** Improves corticospinal tract efficiency, reducing the neural effort required for movement
- **DLPFC stimulation:** Enhances executive function and cognitive fatigue
- **Neuroplasticity:** Promotes compensatory pathways around areas of demyelination

## Evidence

**Motor cortex protocols:**
- Multiple studies show high-frequency rTMS to the motor cortex reduces fatigue severity scores (Fatigue Severity Scale, Modified Fatigue Impact Scale)
- Improvements typically 20-35% in fatigue measures
- Some patients also report improvement in spasticity and walking speed

**DLPFC protocols:**
- Targeting cognitive fatigue and co-occurring depression
- Depression is present in 50% of MS patients and worsens fatigue
- Standard depression TMS protocols effective for the mood component

**Combined approaches:**
- Sequential motor cortex + DLPFC stimulation may address both physical and cognitive fatigue

## Treatment parameters studied

- **Frequency:** 5-20Hz (high-frequency, excitatory)
- **Target:** Primary motor cortex (M1) or DLPFC
- **Sessions:** 10-20 sessions over 2-4 weeks
- **Maintenance:** May need periodic maintenance courses

## Safety in MS patients

- TMS is generally safe in MS — the magnetic field doesn't worsen demyelination
- MS lesions near the cortex don't increase seizure risk from TMS
- Patients with implanted baclofen pumps should be evaluated (metal near treatment site)
- Fatigue from the sessions themselves (traveling to clinic daily) can be a practical barrier
- Heat sensitivity common in MS — ensure the treatment room is cool

## Practical considerations

- Discuss with your MS neurologist — TMS for fatigue is off-label
- The strongest evidence is for motor cortex stimulation, not standard depression protocols
- Look for MS research centers or rehabilitation programs studying TMS
- Insurance does not cover TMS for MS fatigue specifically
- If you also have depression, TMS for depression may be covered and may improve fatigue as a secondary benefit
