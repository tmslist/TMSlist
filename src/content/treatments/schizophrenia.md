## TMS for schizophrenia symptoms

Schizophrenia involves positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) and negative symptoms (flat affect, avolition, social withdrawal). Antipsychotics treat positive symptoms effectively but leave negative symptoms largely untouched. TMS research targets both — with different brain regions for each.

TMS is **not FDA-approved** for schizophrenia, but it is one of the most studied psychiatric applications after depression.

## Auditory hallucinations

The strongest evidence for TMS in schizophrenia is for treatment-resistant auditory verbal hallucinations (AVH) — "hearing voices."

**Target:** Left temporoparietal junction (TPJ) — the brain region overactive during hallucinations

**Protocol:** Low-frequency (1Hz) rTMS to reduce TPJ hyperactivity

**Evidence:**
- A 2020 meta-analysis of 23 RCTs found low-frequency rTMS to the left TPJ significantly reduced hallucination severity
- About 30-50% of patients experience meaningful reduction in voice hearing
- Effects last 2-8 weeks after treatment course
- Works best as add-on to antipsychotic medication, not replacement

## Negative symptoms

**Target:** Left DLPFC — the same region targeted in depression (underactive in schizophrenia negative symptoms)

**Protocol:** High-frequency (10-20Hz) rTMS to increase DLPFC activity

**Evidence:**
- Results are mixed — some studies show improvement in motivation, social function, and emotional expression
- A 2021 meta-analysis found a small but significant effect on negative symptoms
- Effects are modest and may require maintenance treatment
- This is a harder treatment target than hallucinations

## Treatment protocols

| Target | Frequency | Region | Sessions | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|--------|----------|----------|
| Hallucinations | 1Hz (inhibitory) | Left TPJ | 10-20 | Strong |
| Negative symptoms | 10-20Hz (excitatory) | Left DLPFC | 20-30 | Moderate |
| Both | Sequential bilateral | TPJ + DLPFC | 20+ | Emerging |

## Important safety considerations

- TMS lowers seizure threshold — relevant for patients on clozapine or other medications that also lower seizure threshold
- Close coordination with prescribing psychiatrist is essential
- Not a substitute for antipsychotic medication
- Patients with disorganized symptoms may have difficulty tolerating sessions
- Paranoid symptoms may complicate the therapeutic relationship with TMS staff

## Who might benefit

TMS for schizophrenia may be worth discussing with your psychiatrist if:
- Auditory hallucinations persist despite adequate antipsychotic trials
- Negative symptoms significantly impair functioning
- You cannot tolerate higher doses of antipsychotics
- You're willing to try an adjunctive (add-on) treatment

## Finding treatment

This is a specialized application. Look for:
- Academic psychiatry departments with schizophrenia research programs
- Clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov: "TMS schizophrenia")
- University-affiliated TMS labs studying psychotic disorders
- Do not discontinue or adjust antipsychotic medications based on TMS response without psychiatrist guidance
