## What is Anxiety and How TMS Helps

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions in the U.S. — about 40 million adults every year. Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), social anxiety, panic disorder. They wreck sleep, strain relationships, and make work feel impossible. Persistent worry. Physical tension. Avoidance of things you used to do without thinking.

What's going on in the brain? The prefrontal cortex is supposed to act as a brake on the amygdala's fear responses — it checks whether a threat is real and, if it's not, sends calming signals. In anxiety disorders, that brake is weak. The amygdala fires excessively, and the prefrontal cortex can't rein it in. Your brain gets stuck in high alert even when nothing dangerous is happening.

Standard treatments — SSRIs, SNRIs, benzodiazepines, buspirone, CBT — work for a lot of people. But 30-40% don't get adequate relief. Benzodiazepines carry dependence and cognitive impairment risks. SSRIs and SNRIs can cause sexual dysfunction, weight gain, and emotional flattening. If you've cycled through these options without real results, the menu gets thin fast.

TMS goes after anxiety at its source. By applying inhibitory stimulation to overactive brain circuits — or by strengthening the prefrontal cortex's ability to regulate the amygdala — TMS reduces the neural hyperactivity driving your symptoms. It doesn't enter your bloodstream. No dependence risk. No systemic side effects.

## How TMS Works for Anxiety

Here's where anxiety and depression protocols diverge. Depression TMS targets the left DLPFC with excitatory high-frequency stimulation to boost underactive circuits. Anxiety flips the script: the most studied protocol uses low-frequency (1 Hz) stimulation on the right DLPFC. Low-frequency rTMS has an inhibitory effect — it dials down neural activity. Since anxiety involves overactivity in right prefrontal circuits linked to threat processing and negative emotion, dampening that activity can turn down the volume on anxious thoughts and physical arousal.

Another option: continuous theta burst stimulation (cTBS) on the right DLPFC. Also inhibitory, but dramatically shorter — about 40 seconds of active stimulation vs. 20-30 minutes for standard 1 Hz protocols.

Some clinicians use bilateral protocols that do both in one session: low-frequency on the right, then high-frequency on the left. This makes particular sense because anxiety and depression co-occur in roughly 60% of patients. Why treat one when you can address both?

Stimulation runs at 110-120% of your motor threshold. Sessions last 20-40 minutes depending on protocol. Treatment courses range from 20 to 36 sessions over 4-6 weeks.

| Factor | Depression Protocol | Anxiety Protocol |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Target | Left DLPFC | Right DLPFC |
| Frequency | High (10 Hz or iTBS) | Low (1 Hz) or cTBS |
| Goal | Increase activity | Decrease activity |
| Sessions | 30-36 standard | 20-36 (varies by study) |
| Session length | 19-37 minutes | 20-40 minutes |

## Clinical Evidence and Success Rates

TMS is not FDA-cleared for any anxiety disorder as of 2025. But it's increasingly used off-label, backed by a growing stack of clinical evidence.

What the research shows:

- A 2019 meta-analysis in the Journal of Affective Disorders looked at 14 randomized controlled trials of rTMS for GAD. Active TMS beat sham, with response rates between 40-55%.
- A 2021 study in Brain Stimulation showed that low-frequency right DLPFC stimulation produced meaningful reductions on the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAM-A), with effects still holding at 4-week follow-up.
- Here's a useful finding: patients getting standard left DLPFC TMS for depression frequently report significant anxiety reduction as a bonus. A 2020 analysis of NeuroStar outcomes data found that 60% of patients with both anxiety and depression saw meaningful improvement in anxiety scores alongside their depression improvement.
- Bilateral protocols (right then left DLPFC stimulation) show particularly strong results when anxiety and depression overlap, with combined response rates above 50% in several studies.

The most practical path right now? If you have both anxiety and depression — and about 60% of anxiety patients do — TMS can be approved through the depression indication, and your anxiety often improves too. Researchers are pursuing FDA clearance specifically for GAD, but the required large multi-site trials are still in progress.

## Who Qualifies for TMS Treatment

Because TMS isn't FDA-cleared for anxiety, qualification criteria aren't as standardized as for depression. Generally, TMS for anxiety fits best for:

- **Patients with both anxiety and depression**: The most straightforward path. If you meet criteria for treatment-resistant MDD and also have significant anxiety, TMS can be approved for the depression, and your anxiety will likely benefit too.
- **Treatment-resistant GAD**: If you've tried at least 2-3 first-line medications (SSRIs, SNRIs, buspirone) and/or CBT without adequate results, you may be a candidate for off-label TMS.
- **Medication intolerance**: Can't handle anti-anxiety medication side effects — especially benzodiazepine sedation and cognitive impairment, or SSRI sexual dysfunction? TMS offers a different path entirely.

Contraindications are the same as for depression: metallic implants in or near the head, active seizure disorder, and implanted neurostimulation devices.

Age requirements follow the same guidelines — primarily adults 18+, though adolescent use has been studied.

## What to Expect During Treatment

The day-to-day experience is very similar to depression TMS. You sit in a comfortable chair. Outpatient setting. No anesthesia, no sedation, no prep.

A typical course:

- **Sessions**: 20-36 treatments, depending on protocol and your clinician's recommendation
- **Frequency**: 5 days a week (weekdays)
- **Duration**: 4-6 weeks total
- **Session length**: 20-40 minutes, or as short as 3-5 minutes for theta burst

The coil goes against the right side of your head for anxiety-specific protocols, or both sides for bilateral protocols. You'll hear clicking and feel rhythmic tapping on your scalp. Unusual at first. Most people adjust quickly.

Improvement usually starts during weeks 2-3. The early signs are physical: reduced tension, fewer intense worry episodes, better sleep, a general feeling of being less "on edge." Full effects keep building through the course and may continue improving for several weeks after the last session.

After each session, you walk out and go about your day. No cognitive effects, no driving restrictions, no recovery time.

## Side Effects and Safety

Same safety profile as TMS for any other condition:

- **Scalp discomfort** during stimulation — the most common side effect, and it usually fades after the first week
- **Mild headache** after sessions, about 20-30% of patients. Over-the-counter pain relievers handle it.
- **Lightheadedness** — occasional and brief
- **Seizure risk** — extremely rare, less than 0.1%

Now compare that to what anxiety medications can do:

- **No sedation or cognitive impairment** (unlike benzodiazepines)
- **No dependence or withdrawal** (unlike benzodiazepines, which can be brutal to discontinue)
- **No weight gain or sexual dysfunction** (unlike SSRIs and SNRIs)
- **No serotonin syndrome risk** or drug interactions
- **No emotional blunting**

For people who've struggled with benzo dependence or SSRI side effects, TMS's clean profile is often the main reason they seek it out.

## TMS Devices Used for Anxiety

No device has specific FDA clearance for anxiety disorders, so the same systems used for depression are employed off-label:

- **NeuroStar** — Most widely used in the U.S. The figure-8 coil can target either right or left DLPFC. Supports standard rTMS and theta burst.
- **BrainsWay Deep TMS** — The H-coil reaches deeper and broader brain regions. Some clinicians prefer it for anxiety because it may engage more of the prefrontal-limbic circuitry involved in anxiety regulation.
- **MagVenture** — Versatile system supporting multiple coil configurations. Widely used in anxiety research.

Protocol options:

- **Standard low-frequency rTMS** (1 Hz right DLPFC): The most studied approach, 20-30-minute sessions
- **Continuous theta burst (cTBS)** on right DLPFC: Much shorter sessions (under 1 minute of active stimulation), with emerging evidence supporting efficacy
- **Bilateral sequential**: Low-frequency right followed by high-frequency left in one session, 30-45 minutes total
- **Deep TMS bilateral protocols**: H-coils for broader coverage

## Cost and Insurance Coverage

This is the hard part. No FDA clearance for anxiety means insurance generally won't cover anxiety-only TMS.

But there are workarounds:

- **Comorbid depression**: If you have both anxiety and depression (roughly 60% of anxiety patients do), TMS can often be approved under the depression indication. Your anxiety will likely improve along with it.
- **Out-of-pocket**: A full course typically runs $6,000-$12,000. Per-session costs range from $200-$400.
- **Cash-pay discounts**: Many clinics offer reduced rates for self-pay patients, plus financing or payment plans.
- **Clinical trials**: Active research studies sometimes provide TMS for anxiety at no cost. Check ClinicalTrials.gov for studies near you.

If you're going the insurance route through a depression diagnosis, the same documentation applies: at least 2 failed medication trials, a letter of medical necessity, and formal prior authorization.

## Finding a TMS Provider for Anxiety

Not every TMS clinic has experience treating anxiety. When you're searching, ask these questions:

- **Have you treated anxiety patients with TMS?** You want a clinic with explicit experience using right DLPFC or bilateral protocols — not just standard depression protocols repackaged.
- **Which protocol do you use for anxiety?** A knowledgeable provider should be able to discuss low-frequency right DLPFC, bilateral approaches, or theta burst options without hesitation.
- **Do you track anxiety outcomes separately?** Good clinics measure anxiety with validated scales (GAD-7 or HAM-A) alongside depression measures.
- **What's your approach for patients with both anxiety and depression?** The answer should involve bilateral protocols or sequential treatment strategies.

Red flags:

- Clinics that only offer left DLPFC depression protocols and claim they'll handle anxiety
- No familiarity with right-sided or bilateral stimulation
- Can't discuss the specific evidence for TMS in anxiety
- No validated anxiety measurement tools during treatment

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is TMS FDA-approved for anxiety?**
No. As of 2025, TMS isn't FDA-cleared for any anxiety disorder. It's used off-label based on growing clinical evidence. The strongest insurance pathway is through a comorbid depression diagnosis — which most anxiety patients have.

**Can TMS make anxiety worse before it gets better?**
Some people report a brief increase in anxiety during the first few sessions, particularly with protocols that include high-frequency left DLPFC stimulation. This usually resolves within the first week. If your primary issue is anxiety without depression, make sure your provider uses an anxiety-appropriate protocol (right DLPFC inhibitory stimulation) rather than a standard depression protocol. That distinction matters.

**How long do the benefits last?**
Anxiety improvements from TMS typically last several months after treatment. Some people maintain benefits for 6-12 months; others need periodic maintenance sessions. Durability varies by individual and the severity of the underlying anxiety.

**Will my insurance cover TMS for anxiety?**
Insurance generally won't cover TMS for anxiety alone. But if you also have treatment-resistant depression, coverage can usually be obtained through the depression indication. Most people with significant anxiety also qualify for a depression diagnosis.

**Can I continue my anxiety medication during TMS?**
Yes. TMS can be safely combined with SSRIs, SNRIs, buspirone, and most other anxiety medications. Your psychiatrist should coordinate with your TMS provider on any adjustments. Benzodiazepines are also generally compatible, though some providers prefer to minimize them during treatment to better gauge TMS response.
