## How TMS addresses anxiety

Anxiety disorders involve overactivity in certain brain circuits, particularly those connecting the prefrontal cortex to the amygdala. While TMS for depression targets the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) with excitatory stimulation, anxiety protocols typically focus on the right DLPFC using inhibitory stimulation. The goal is to calm overactive circuits rather than boost underactive ones.

This distinction matters. Depression and anxiety often involve opposite patterns of brain activity in the prefrontal cortex, and the treatment approach reflects that.

## FDA status: off-label but well-studied

TMS is not FDA-cleared specifically for anxiety disorders. However, it is widely used off-label for generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), and there is a meaningful body of clinical evidence supporting its use.

Several controlled trials have shown significant reductions in anxiety symptoms using low-frequency (1 Hz) right DLPFC stimulation. Response rates in these studies typically fall between 40-55%, though the research base is smaller than what exists for depression.

The strongest evidence exists for patients who have both anxiety and depression. Since TMS is FDA-cleared for depression, clinicians can treat the depression directly while the anxiety often improves as well. In fact, many patients who undergo standard left DLPFC depression treatment report meaningful anxiety reduction as a secondary benefit.

## Who this works best for

TMS for anxiety tends to show the most benefit in these situations:

- **Comorbid anxiety and depression**: If you have both conditions, TMS treatment for depression frequently reduces anxiety symptoms too. This is the easiest path to insurance coverage.
- **Treatment-resistant GAD**: Patients who have tried multiple SSRIs, SNRIs, and buspirone without adequate relief may find TMS helpful as an adjunct or alternative.
- **Medication intolerance**: Some people experience intolerable side effects from anti-anxiety medications. TMS avoids the sedation, dependence risk, and cognitive dulling associated with benzodiazepines.

## How anxiety protocols differ from depression protocols

| Factor | Depression Protocol | Anxiety Protocol |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Target | Left DLPFC | Right DLPFC |
| Frequency | High (10 Hz or iTBS) | Low (1 Hz) or cTBS |
| Goal | Increase activity | Decrease activity |
| Sessions | 30-36 standard | 20-36 (varies by study) |

Some clinicians use bilateral protocols that stimulate both sides in a single session — low-frequency on the right followed by high-frequency on the left. This approach may address anxiety and depression simultaneously.

## What to expect during treatment

The practical experience is very similar to TMS for depression. You sit in a chair, a coil is positioned against your head, and you feel rhythmic tapping or clicking during each pulse sequence. Sessions last 20-40 minutes depending on the protocol.

Side effects are the same as standard TMS: scalp discomfort during treatment and occasional mild headaches afterward. These tend to diminish after the first week.

## Insurance and cost considerations

This is where things get complicated. Because TMS for anxiety alone is off-label, most insurance plans will not cover it as a standalone anxiety treatment. However, if you have a comorbid depression diagnosis that meets coverage criteria, treatment can often be approved under the depression indication.

Out-of-pocket costs for a full treatment course typically range from $6,000 to $12,000 without insurance. Some clinics offer cash-pay discounts or financing plans.

## The bottom line

TMS for anxiety is not yet FDA-cleared, but the clinical evidence is encouraging, particularly for patients who also have depression. If you have treatment-resistant anxiety, it is worth discussing with a psychiatrist who specializes in TMS. The risk profile is low, the side effects are minimal, and the potential benefit is real — especially if conventional medications have not worked for you.

Ask your provider specifically about right DLPFC protocols and whether a bilateral approach might make sense for your symptoms.
