## How TMS works for migraines

Migraines involve a wave of abnormal electrical activity that spreads across the brain's cortex, called cortical spreading depression. This wave is what causes the aura that some migraine sufferers experience — visual disturbances, tingling, or other sensory changes — and it triggers the cascade of events that produces migraine pain.

Single-pulse TMS (sTMS) interrupts this process. A single, brief magnetic pulse disrupts the wave of cortical spreading depression before it can fully develop, effectively stopping the migraine in its tracks or preventing it from becoming severe.

This is fundamentally different from how TMS is used for psychiatric conditions. Depression treatment uses repetitive pulses over many sessions to gradually change brain activity patterns. Migraine treatment uses individual pulses applied at the onset of symptoms for immediate effect.

## The devices

The primary FDA-cleared TMS device for migraines is the SpringTMS (now marketed as sTMS mini), a portable, handheld device you use at home. This is important — unlike TMS for depression, you do not need to visit a clinic for every treatment.

The device is placed against the back of the head (over the occipital cortex) and delivers a single magnetic pulse when you press a button. It is designed to be simple enough to use during the early stages of a migraine attack.

## Acute treatment vs. prevention

TMS for migraines serves two distinct purposes:

### Acute treatment (stopping an attack)
When you feel a migraine coming on — especially if you notice aura symptoms — you apply the device and deliver pulses. Clinical data shows:

- **39% of patients** were pain-free at 2 hours (compared to 22% with sham)
- **34% had sustained pain relief** at 24 hours without rescue medication
- Works best when used early, ideally during the aura phase

### Preventive treatment (reducing frequency)
Used on a regular schedule — typically 4 pulses twice daily — sTMS can reduce the number of migraine days per month. Studies have shown:

- Reduction of 2-3 migraine days per month compared to baseline
- Decreased need for acute migraine medications
- Cumulative benefit over several months of consistent use

## Who benefits most

TMS for migraines is most effective for:

- **Migraine with aura**: This is the population with the strongest evidence. The FDA clearance specifically covers migraine with aura, and the mechanism of action (disrupting cortical spreading depression) directly addresses the aura process.
- **Patients who want fewer medications**: If you are concerned about overuse headaches from triptans, or want to reduce your medication burden, sTMS offers a drug-free alternative.
- **Adolescents**: The favorable safety profile makes TMS a reasonable option for younger patients where medication options are more limited.

It may also help migraine without aura, though the evidence is not as strong. The same cortical mechanisms are involved, just less visibly.

## Side effects

The side effect profile for sTMS is remarkably mild:

- **Lightheadedness** in a small percentage of users (usually transient)
- **Tingling** at the application site
- No systemic side effects — nothing comparable to the fatigue, cognitive fog, or weight gain associated with preventive migraine medications

The main contraindication is the same as for all TMS: do not use if you have metallic implants in or near the head, or a history of seizures.

## sTMS vs. rTMS for migraines

| Feature | sTMS (single-pulse) | rTMS (repetitive) |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| FDA status for migraines | Cleared | Not specifically cleared |
| Setting | Home use | Clinic-based |
| Mechanism | Interrupts cortical spreading depression | Modulates pain processing networks |
| Use | Acute and preventive | Investigational for chronic migraine |
| Evidence | Strong for migraine with aura | Growing but less established |

Some researchers are studying repetitive TMS (rTMS) protocols — similar to those used for depression — for chronic migraine prevention. This involves clinic-based sessions targeting the motor cortex or DLPFC. The research is promising but not yet at the stage of FDA clearance.

## Getting access

To obtain an sTMS device, you need a prescription from a healthcare provider. The process typically involves:

1. **Diagnosis** of migraine with aura by a neurologist or headache specialist
2. **Prescription** for the sTMS device
3. **Training** on how to use the device at home
4. **Insurance or rental**: Some patients rent the device monthly. Coverage varies by plan — check with your insurer and be prepared to appeal if initially denied.

## The bottom line

TMS for migraines is one of the few drug-free, FDA-cleared options for both treating acute attacks and reducing migraine frequency. The evidence is strongest for migraine with aura, and the home-use format makes it far more practical than clinic-based treatments. If you are managing migraines and want to reduce your reliance on medications, ask your neurologist about single-pulse TMS.
