## Why TMS appeals to new mothers

The biggest advantage of TMS for postpartum depression is simple: it doesn't enter your bloodstream. For breastfeeding mothers concerned about passing medication to their baby, TMS offers an effective depression treatment with zero systemic exposure.

TMS isn't specifically FDA-cleared for postpartum depression, but it is cleared for MDD — and postpartum depression is clinically classified as MDD with peripartum onset. Most psychiatrists treat it the same way.

## Safety during breastfeeding

TMS is considered safe during breastfeeding because:
- No medication enters the bloodstream — the magnetic field acts only on the brain
- No anesthesia or sedation — you're fully alert
- The magnetic field doesn't reach breast tissue
- No known effects on breast milk composition

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recognizes TMS as an option for treatment-resistant peripartum depression.

## Practical considerations for new parents

- **Childcare**: You'll need someone to watch the baby during sessions (20-37 minutes, 5 days/week for 6 weeks)
- **Timing**: Start as early as possible. Postpartum depression left untreated affects bonding, infant development, and partner relationships
- **Insurance**: Covered under standard MDD criteria. The "postpartum" label doesn't change coverage
- **Scheduling**: Many clinics accommodate early morning sessions so you're back home before the baby's next feed

## When to consider TMS

- You've been diagnosed with postpartum depression
- You're reluctant to take antidepressants while breastfeeding
- You've tried an SSRI and it hasn't worked or causes intolerable side effects
- Your depression is moderate to severe and affecting your ability to care for your baby

Don't wait to see if it passes on its own. Postpartum depression that lasts beyond 2 weeks warrants treatment, and TMS can begin while you explore other options.
