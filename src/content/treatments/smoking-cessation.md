## How TMS helps with smoking cessation

Nicotine addiction is driven by the brain's reward circuits, particularly the insula and prefrontal cortex. The insula plays a critical role in craving — studies have shown that damage to the insula can cause smokers to quit effortlessly, losing the urge to smoke almost overnight. TMS targets these same circuits non-invasively, reducing the intensity of cravings and making it easier to resist the pull of nicotine.

The FDA cleared BrainsWay's Deep TMS system for smoking cessation in 2020. This was a significant milestone because it validated a non-pharmacological approach to addiction treatment.

## How it works

The BrainsWay system uses an H-coil helmet that delivers magnetic pulses to deeper brain structures than standard figure-8 coils can reach. For smoking cessation, the protocol uses bilateral stimulation targeting the lateral prefrontal cortex and insula simultaneously.

Here is the key detail that makes this protocol different: each treatment session includes a cue-exposure component. Before the magnetic stimulation begins, you are presented with smoking-related cues — typically a short video or images involving cigarettes and smoking. This intentionally activates your craving circuits right before TMS modulates them, which appears to enhance the treatment effect.

Think of it as catching the craving circuits in the act and then applying the brakes.

## What the numbers show

The pivotal trial that led to FDA clearance produced these results:

| Measure | TMS Group | Sham Group |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Quit rate at end of treatment | 28% | 12% |
| Continuous abstinence at 18 weeks | 33% | 16% |
| Cigarette consumption reduction | Significant | Modest |

These numbers may seem modest compared to TMS response rates for depression, but context matters. Smoking cessation is notoriously difficult. Any treatment that roughly doubles quit rates compared to placebo is clinically meaningful, especially for people who have failed other approaches.

## What a treatment course looks like

- **Sessions**: Typically 18 sessions over 6 weeks (3 sessions per week)
- **Session length**: About 20 minutes of active stimulation, plus cue exposure
- **Protocol**: High-frequency (10 Hz) bilateral deep TMS
- **During treatment**: You watch a brief smoking-related video, then receive the TMS pulses. You may feel tapping or pressure on your scalp.
- **After**: No downtime. You can return to normal activities immediately.

## Who is a good candidate

TMS for smoking cessation may be right for you if:

- **You have tried and failed** other quit methods — nicotine replacement therapy, varenicline (Chantix), bupropion (Wellbutrin), counseling, or a combination
- **You want to avoid medication side effects** — some people cannot tolerate the nausea, insomnia, or mood changes associated with cessation drugs
- **You are motivated to quit** — TMS reduces cravings, but it works best when combined with a genuine commitment to stopping

TMS is not a magic bullet. It lowers the intensity of cravings and makes quitting more achievable, but you still need to put in the effort.

## Insurance coverage

Despite FDA clearance, insurance coverage for TMS smoking cessation is inconsistent. Many plans do not yet cover it, and prior authorization can be difficult to obtain. The situation is evolving, but as of now, many patients pay out of pocket.

A full course typically costs between $6,000 and $10,000 without insurance. Some clinics offer package pricing or financing. If you have a comorbid condition like depression that is also being treated with TMS, your provider may be able to structure the treatment plan in a way that improves coverage prospects.

## Combining TMS with other approaches

TMS works best as part of a comprehensive quit plan. Most clinicians recommend combining it with:

- **Behavioral counseling** or a structured cessation program
- **Nicotine replacement therapy** (patches, gum, or lozenges) if appropriate
- **Ongoing support** through the weeks and months following treatment

The goal is to use TMS to take the edge off cravings while you build new habits and coping strategies.

## The bottom line

TMS for smoking cessation is FDA-cleared, safe, and roughly doubles quit rates compared to sham treatment. It targets the specific brain circuits that drive nicotine cravings, using a cue-exposure approach that is unique among TMS protocols. If you have struggled to quit smoking through other methods, it is a legitimate option worth discussing with a TMS provider — just be prepared for the possibility of paying out of pocket.
