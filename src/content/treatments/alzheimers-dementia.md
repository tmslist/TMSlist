## Can TMS help with Alzheimer's?

Alzheimer's disease (AD) involves progressive neuronal loss and synaptic dysfunction, particularly in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. TMS cannot reverse neurodegeneration, but research suggests it may temporarily enhance remaining neural function and promote compensatory brain plasticity.

TMS is **not FDA-approved** for Alzheimer's or any form of dementia.

## How TMS is being applied

**Cognitive enhancement protocols:**
- High-frequency rTMS to the left DLPFC to improve executive function
- Stimulation of parietal regions involved in memory networks
- Multi-site protocols targeting 6 brain regions across treatment sessions (developed by Nexstim)

**The neuroAD approach:**
A combined TMS + cognitive training protocol (developed by Neuronix) targets specific brain regions while simultaneously engaging those regions with cognitive exercises. This dual approach is the most studied for AD.

## What the evidence shows

- A 2023 meta-analysis of 16 studies found rTMS produced modest but significant improvements in cognitive scores (ADAS-Cog, MMSE) in mild-moderate AD
- Benefits were larger with multi-session protocols (20+ sessions) and combined TMS + cognitive training
- Improvements in daily functioning and caregiver-rated outcomes reported in some studies
- Effects typically last 1-3 months after treatment; maintenance sessions may extend benefit
- Less effective for severe AD — neural substrate too degraded

## Limitations

- Effects are modest — don't expect dramatic cognitive recovery
- Not a cure or disease-modifying treatment
- Benefits may be temporary without maintenance
- Large, definitive trials are still ongoing
- Insurance does not cover TMS for dementia

## Who might consider TMS

- Patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) or mild-moderate AD
- Those already on standard AD medications (donepezil, memantine) with incomplete response
- Families seeking adjunctive approaches to slow functional decline
- Patients who can cooperate with repeated sessions (20-30 over several weeks)

## Practical guidance

- Discuss with your neurologist before pursuing TMS for dementia
- Look for clinical trials at academic memory disorder centers
- Be cautious of clinics marketing TMS as an Alzheimer's treatment — the evidence supports potential benefit, not proven efficacy
- Combined TMS + cognitive training may offer more benefit than TMS alone
