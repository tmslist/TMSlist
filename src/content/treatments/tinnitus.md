## How TMS targets tinnitus

Tinnitus involves hyperactivity in the auditory cortex — your brain generates a sound signal even when there's no external source. TMS for tinnitus uses low-frequency (inhibitory) stimulation over the left temporoparietal cortex to calm this overactivity.

This is a different target and protocol than depression treatment. Not every TMS clinic can treat tinnitus — you need a provider experienced with temporal lobe targeting.

## What the evidence shows

Results are mixed but cautiously promising:
- About **30-40%** of patients report meaningful reduction in tinnitus loudness or distress
- Effects tend to be more pronounced for tinnitus of shorter duration (<3 years)
- Multiple treatment courses may be needed
- Some patients experience only temporary relief (days to weeks)

A 2023 Cochrane review concluded there's "low to moderate quality evidence" supporting TMS for tinnitus, with the caveat that optimal protocols haven't been standardized yet.

## What to expect

- **Target**: Left temporoparietal junction (not DLPFC like depression)
- **Protocol**: Low-frequency (1 Hz) inhibitory stimulation
- **Sessions**: Typically 10-20 sessions over 2-4 weeks
- **Insurance**: Not covered (off-label). Expect to pay $3,000-$8,000

## Finding a provider

Ask specifically: "Have you treated tinnitus patients with TMS?" Many depression-focused TMS clinics don't have experience with temporal lobe protocols. Academic medical centers with neurology departments are more likely to offer this.
