## How TMS targets eating disorders

Eating disorders like anorexia nervosa (AN) and bulimia nervosa (BN) involve dysregulated activity in brain circuits controlling reward, impulse control, and body image perception. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) — the same region targeted in TMS for depression — plays a central role in cognitive control over eating behaviors.

Research suggests that patients with eating disorders often show reduced DLPFC activity, which may contribute to difficulty resisting compulsive behaviors (bingeing, purging, restriction). TMS aims to restore normal activity in these circuits.

## Current research status

TMS for eating disorders is **not FDA-approved** and remains in the research phase. However, several clinical studies show promising results:

- A 2023 meta-analysis of 14 studies found high-frequency rTMS to the left DLPFC significantly reduced binge eating episodes and food cravings
- Studies in anorexia nervosa show TMS may reduce anxiety around eating and improve cognitive flexibility
- Deep TMS (H-coil) targeting the insula and prefrontal cortex is being investigated for body image distortion

## What the studies show

**For bulimia nervosa and binge eating disorder:**
- Reduction in binge/purge frequency by 40-60% in some trials
- Decreased food craving intensity
- Effects appear strongest when combined with psychotherapy (CBT)

**For anorexia nervosa:**
- Reduced anxiety and rigidity around food
- Some improvement in BMI in chronic, severe cases
- Research is earlier-stage; sample sizes are small

## Treatment protocols being studied

- **Standard rTMS:** 10Hz stimulation to left DLPFC, 20-30 sessions
- **Deep TMS:** H-coil targeting broader networks, 20 sessions
- **Theta burst stimulation:** Accelerated protocols being explored

## Important considerations

- TMS for eating disorders should be part of a comprehensive treatment plan including nutritional rehabilitation, psychotherapy, and medical monitoring
- Patients with very low BMI may need medical stabilization before TMS
- Most eating disorder TMS studies have been small; larger randomized controlled trials are needed
- Insurance does not currently cover TMS for eating disorder indications

## Finding a provider

Because TMS for eating disorders is still investigational, look for academic medical centers or specialized eating disorder programs that offer TMS as part of a clinical trial. Some private TMS clinics may offer off-label treatment, but ensure the provider has experience with eating disorder populations.
