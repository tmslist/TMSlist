# Stanford TMS Clinic: Where the Future of TMS Is Being Written

**Stanford's TMS program** isn't just a clinic. It's where the **Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT)** protocol was born — the one that compressed six weeks of treatment into five days and produced remission rates that made researchers around the world stop what they were doing and pay attention.

That's the draw. But cutting-edge research and smooth day-to-day patient access? Those don't always live in the same building.

Here's what it's actually like to be a patient at Stanford's TMS program in 2026.

## The Bottom Line

Unmatched expertise. If you can tolerate the academic medical center logistics, the care is exceptional — especially for complex cases that other clinics won't take on.

## The SNT Protocol: Why People Fly Across the Country for This

The **Stanford Neuromodulation Therapy (SNT)** protocol is the single biggest breakthrough in TMS treatment in the past decade. Full stop. Developed by Dr. Nolan Williams and his team at the Stanford Brain Stimulation Lab, it rethinks nearly everything about how TMS is delivered:

- **Five days, not six weeks.** You receive 10 sessions per day for 5 consecutive days. Fifty total sessions in one week.
- **fMRI-guided targeting.** Instead of using anatomical landmarks to find the treatment spot (the "measure from the nose" approach), they use functional MRI to map your individual brain and find the exact target within the left DLPFC that will respond best.
- **Higher-dose theta burst.** Intermittent theta burst stimulation at doses above what standard protocols use.
- **The results.** The landmark 2022 study in the American Journal of Psychiatry showed a **79% remission rate.** Not response. Remission. That's roughly 2.5 times what standard TMS achieves.

Let that number sit for a second. 79%.

### Who Gets Access?

As of 2026, Stanford offers SNT in two tracks:

- **Research:** Ongoing clinical trials studying SNT for depression, PTSD, bipolar depression, and suicidal ideation. Trial participants get treatment at no cost.
- **Clinical:** SNT is available as a paid clinical service for eligible patients. Insurance coverage for the accelerated format is still uneven — some plans cover it, others need convincing.

The fMRI targeting adds cost and scheduling complexity. Not everyone qualifies. And the five-day intensive format means clearing your entire schedule for a week. No halfway.

## Standard TMS at Stanford

Not everyone needs — or qualifies for — the SNT protocol. Stanford also offers conventional TMS:

- **Standard rTMS:** 36 sessions over 6 weeks on NeuroStar or MagVenture
- **Theta burst:** 3-minute sessions for qualifying patients
- **Maintenance:** Taper and ongoing sessions after your initial course

The clinical team includes attending psychiatrists, fellows, and residents. You benefit from top-tier oversight, but you'll interact with trainees during some sessions. If you're comfortable in a teaching hospital, this is fine. If seeing a different face throws you off, know that going in.

## What Patients Are Saying

We went through about 60 reviews and patient accounts from Google, Stanford Health Care surveys, and TMS community forums.

**What people value most:**
- **Confidence.** The most common theme. People describe feeling like they're "in the best hands possible" — that the team doesn't just follow a protocol, they understand the neuroscience underneath it.
- **Clinical trial access.** Several people who couldn't afford TMS or whose insurance denied them were enrolled in trials and received treatment at no cost. That path simply doesn't exist at a private clinic.
- **SNT results speak for themselves.** People who went through the accelerated protocol describe rapid, sometimes dramatic improvement. The words "miraculous" and "life-saving" appear in multiple reviews. People don't use those words casually.
- **Thorough intake.** Stanford's evaluation is detailed. They look at the full clinical picture before recommending a protocol. Nobody's rushing you through a checklist.

**What frustrates people:**
- **The wait.** Number one complaint. Getting an initial consultation can take 3-8 weeks. Once authorized, treatment start might be another 1-2 weeks. If you're in crisis, that timeline is hard.
- **Academic center complexity.** Scheduling, billing, referrals — dealing with Stanford Health Care's systems is noticeably harder than calling a private practice. Multiple people described making several calls just to coordinate between departments.
- **Narrow insurance network.** Stanford doesn't contract with as many plans as private clinics. If your HMO isn't Stanford-affiliated, you could face steep out-of-network costs.
- **Palo Alto commute.** The campus is in Palo Alto. If you're coming from San Francisco, San Jose, or the East Bay, you're driving 45 minutes to an hour. Every day. For six weeks. (The SNT protocol's five-day format actually solves this problem nicely.)

## Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Birthplace of the SNT protocol — the most effective TMS approach published to date
- fMRI-guided targeting that personalizes treatment to your specific brain
- Multiple TMS technologies on-site (NeuroStar, MagVenture, BrainsWay)
- Access to cutting-edge clinical trials at no cost
- The treating team includes people who are literally defining the future of this field
- Willing to take on complex, treatment-resistant cases that private clinics may turn away

**Cons:**
- Longer wait times than any private clinic (3-8 weeks for intake)
- Academic bureaucracy adds real friction to scheduling and billing
- Narrower insurance network
- Palo Alto location creates a daily commute problem for most of the Bay Area
- Teaching hospital means trainees, not just attendings
- SNT availability can be limited, and insurance coverage for it is inconsistent

## FAQ

### Can anyone get the SNT protocol?
Not automatically. The Stanford TMS team evaluates you and decides candidacy based on your clinical history, diagnosis, and other factors. Some people are directed to clinical trials. Others receive SNT as a clinical service. You don't just sign up.

### Is Stanford worth the wait versus a private clinic?
For straightforward treatment-resistant depression, a nearby private clinic will get you started faster and deliver comparable results on standard protocols. Stanford is worth the wait if you want the SNT protocol specifically, have a complex clinical picture that needs advanced expertise, or want access to research.

### Does Stanford accept my insurance?
Stanford Health Care takes Medicare, many PPO plans, and select HMOs. Their network is narrower than most private clinics — that's the trade-off for academic medicine. Call Stanford Health Care Financial Services to verify your plan before scheduling anything.

### Can I travel to Stanford for just the 5-day SNT protocol?
Yes, and people do. The intensive format works well for traveling patients. Stanford doesn't arrange lodging, but there are plenty of hotels near campus. Some people fly in for the week.
