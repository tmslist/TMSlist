## They're not competing — they're for different situations

Let's get this out of the way: TMS isn't "better" than medication. Medication isn't "better" than TMS. They serve different situations.

**Antidepressants are the right first step for most people.** They're accessible, affordable (generics cost $4-$30/month), and work well enough for about 60-70% of patients with depression.

**TMS is for when medication hasn't worked.** If you've tried 2+ antidepressants without adequate relief, that's treatment-resistant depression — and that's exactly what TMS was designed for.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Factor | Antidepressants | TMS Therapy |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| **How it works** | Changes brain chemistry (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine) | Magnetic pulses stimulate underactive brain regions |
| **Effectiveness** | 60-70% response (first-line) | 50-60% response (after meds failed) |
| **Time to work** | 4-8 weeks | 4-6 weeks (36 sessions) |
| **Side effects** | Weight gain, sexual dysfunction, drowsiness, nausea | Scalp discomfort, mild headache |
| **Daily commitment** | Take a pill daily, indefinitely | 5 days/week for 6 weeks, then done |
| **Cost without insurance** | $4-$300/month | $6,000-$12,000 total course |
| **Insurance coverage** | Almost always covered | Covered if criteria met (2+ failed meds) |
| **Stopping** | Tapering required (withdrawal possible) | No tapering; effects persist |
| **Cognitive effects** | Some cause brain fog or dulling | None (may improve cognition) |

## The side effect difference is real

This is where the comparison gets personal. Ask anyone who's been on SSRIs about side effects, and you'll get stories:

- **Weight gain**: 25-40% of patients on SSRIs gain significant weight
- **Sexual dysfunction**: 40-65% experience reduced libido, difficulty with arousal or orgasm
- **Emotional blunting**: "I'm not sad anymore, but I'm not anything"
- **Withdrawal**: Stopping SSRIs causes discontinuation syndrome in up to 50% of patients

TMS side effects are local and temporary: scalp discomfort during the session, maybe a headache after. That's it. No systemic effects because the treatment targets one specific brain region.

## When to consider TMS

You're a good candidate for TMS if:
- You've tried at least 2 antidepressants without enough improvement
- Side effects from medication are significantly impacting your quality of life
- You have treatment-resistant depression (the most common reason)
- You want a treatment that doesn't require daily medication

You should probably stick with medication if:
- Your first or second antidepressant is working well
- You can't commit to 5 days/week for 6 weeks
- Your insurance doesn't cover TMS and cost is a barrier
- You have contraindications for TMS (epilepsy, metal implants in the head)

## Can you do both?

Yes. Many people continue their antidepressant during TMS treatment. Some find they can reduce their medication dose after a successful TMS course. This should always be done with your psychiatrist's guidance — never stop medication on your own.

## The cost conversation

Let's be real: TMS is expensive upfront. A full course runs $6,000-$12,000 without insurance. With insurance, your out-of-pocket depends on your plan — some patients pay nothing beyond their deductible, others pay $1,000-$3,000.

But consider the long-term math: if you've been on brand-name antidepressants for years, spending $200-$300/month, that's $2,400-$3,600/year. Over 5 years, that's $12,000-$18,000 — plus ongoing side effects.

TMS is a one-time investment (with possible maintenance sessions). For many people, the math works out.
