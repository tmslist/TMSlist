Executive Summary

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) represents one of the most promising opportunities in behavioral health entrepreneurship. With FDA clearance for treatment-resistant depression and a rapidly growing body of evidence supporting expanded applications, TMS clinics offer healthcare providers a pathway to differentiated, high-margin revenue.

Market Opportunity

$2.5B+
Global Market Size
15%
Annual Growth
30%
Treatment Response Rate

Why TMS Now?

  • FDA-cleared for major depressive disorder (MDD) and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)
  • Insurance coverage expanding across Medicare and commercial payers
  • Growing demand for non-pharmacological mental health treatments
  • Patent expiration on key technologies opening equipment market
  • Increasing acceptance among psychiatrists and referring providers

Target Market

Primary Targets: Outpatient psychiatry practices looking to add procedural services, solo psychiatrists seeking additional revenue streams, pain clinics expanding into behavioral health, and neurology groups differentiating their practice offerings.

Patient Demographics: Adults aged 25-65 with treatment-resistant depression (failed 1-2 medication trials), patients seeking alternatives to medication, those with intolerable side effects, and individuals preferring non-invasive treatment options.

Revenue Model

TMS generates revenue through per-session billing using CPT codes 90867 (initiation), 90868 (delivery), and 90869 (subsequent sessions). A typical course of treatment consists of 36 sessions over 6-9 weeks.

Break-Even Analysis

With a typical startup investment of $265,000 (mid-range) and monthly operating costs of $46,750, most TMS clinics achieve break-even within 8-14 months post-launch. Clinics that prioritize payer contracting and referral network development in the pre-launch phase typically reach profitability faster.

Equipment & Technology

Selecting the right TMS equipment is one of the most critical decisions in clinic planning. Each platform offers distinct advantages depending on your practice focus, patient volume, and budget.

Device Comparison

FDA-Cleared TMS Devices (2026)

Device Type Lease Cost Treatment Time Best For
NeuroStar Table-mounted, figure-8 coil $65-85K/year 19-37 min Standard rTMS, established protocols
BrainsWay Deep TMS Helmet-based, H-coil $75-95K/year 20 min Deep brain stimulation, OCD, smoking cessation
MagVenture Cart-based, figure-8 coil $70-90K/year 17-37 min Flexible positioning, multiple coil options
Nexstim Neuronavigated, SmartFocus $120-150K/year 90-110 min Precision targeting, research applications

Key Selection Criteria

Insurance Acceptance

  • Verify payer coverage for specific devices
  • NeuroStar and BrainsWay have broadest coverage
  • Document medical necessity properly

Treatment Throughput

  • Consider sessions per day per chair
  • Faster treatment = more patients
  • Balance speed with patient comfort

Training & Support

  • On-site installation and training included
  • Technical support availability
  • Clinical protocol development assistance

Total Cost of Ownership

  • Compare lease vs. purchase options
  • Factor in consumables and maintenance
  • Consider upgrade pathways

EMR & Software Requirements

HIPAA-Compliant EMR Integration

  • Scheduling System: Integration with existing practice management or dedicated TMS scheduling software (e.g., GreenLight Health, TMS Cloud)
  • Documentation: Templates for TMS evaluation, consent, treatment notes, and outcomes tracking
  • Billing Integration: Direct link to clearinghouse for clean claim submission
  • Patient Portal: Secure messaging, appointment scheduling, educational resources
  • Reporting: Outcomes data, session completion rates, payer-specific metrics

Authorization Requirements

Most commercial payers require prior authorization for TMS treatment. Build a robust authorization workflow that includes:

  • Insurance verification at intake
  • Benefits investigation for TMS coverage
  • Prior authorization submission with clinical documentation
  • Appeal processes for denials
  • Superbill generation for out-of-network claims

Compliance & Legal

TMS clinics must navigate a complex regulatory environment. Establishing robust compliance programs from the outset protects your patients, your practice, and your license.

HIPAA Requirements

HIPAA Compliance Checklist

Requirement Action Items
Privacy Rule Patient authorization forms, Notice of Privacy Practices, minimum necessary standard
Security Rule Technical safeguards (encryption, access controls), physical safeguards, administrative safeguards
Business Associate Agreements Execute BAAs with: EMR vendor, billing service, clearinghouse, equipment vendor, IT support
Breach Notification Incident response plan, notification procedures, documentation requirements

State Medical Board Requirements

Key Considerations by State

  • Physician Supervision: Determine if a physician must be on-site during all TMS sessions
  • Scope of Practice: Define what tasks can be delegated to TMS technicians
  • Collaborative Agreement: Some states require formal collaborative practice agreements
  • Continuing Education: Track CME requirements for supervising physicians

FDA Reporting & Insurance

FDA Adverse Event Reporting

  • Report serious adverse events to FDA MedWatch
  • Document all device malfunctions
  • Maintain internal adverse event log
  • Follow manufacturer safety communications

Malpractice Insurance

  • Medical malpractice coverage ($1-3M per occurrence)
  • General liability insurance
  • Professional liability for each provider
  • Coverage for TMS-specific procedures

Marketing & Patient Acquisition

A strategic marketing approach focused on provider referrals and patient education will drive sustainable patient volume. Unlike many healthcare services, TMS relies heavily on physician referrals rather than direct-to-consumer advertising.

Referral Network Strategy

Psychiatrists

  • Target those with large MDD patient panels
  • Offer lunch-and-learn presentations
  • Provide seamless referral workflow

Primary Care

  • PCPs often manage initial depression treatment
  • Build relationships with family medicine
  • Provide clear clinical criteria

Nurse Practitioners

  • Growing role in mental health
  • Often have prescriptive authority
  • Can be referral sources and employees

Timeline & Milestones

A realistic timeline helps manage expectations and ensures all critical path items are completed before seeing patients. Most clinics require 10-12 months from planning to first patient.

Glossary of Key Terms

ADA
Americans with Disabilities Act — Federal law requiring accessibility accommodations
BAA
Business Associate Agreement — Contract required under HIPAA for vendors handling PHI
Break-Even
Point at which total revenue equals total costs; no profit or loss
Capital Expenditure
Major investment in long-term assets (equipment, property) vs. operating expenses
CPT Code
Current Procedural Terminology — Standardized codes for medical services billing
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization
FFS
Fee-For-Service — Payment model based on individual services rendered
FQHC
Federally Qualified Health Center — Community-based healthcare provider
GPU
Graphics Processing Unit — Sometimes referenced in TMS neuronavigation systems
LCD
Local Coverage Determination — Medicare policy defining covered services by region
Margin
Profit expressed as percentage of revenue (Gross Margin, Net Margin)
NCD
National Coverage Determination — Medicare national policy for covered services
Operating Expenses
Day-to-day costs of running a business (rent, salaries, supplies)
Payer Mix
Composition of revenue by payer type (Medicare, Commercial, Self-Pay)
Revenue Cycle
End-to-end billing process from patient registration to payment collection
ROI
Return on Investment — Measure of profitability relative to investment cost
Superbill
Itemized form used to submit claims to insurance; includes diagnoses and procedures
Working Capital
Funds available for day-to-day operations; current assets minus current liabilities
