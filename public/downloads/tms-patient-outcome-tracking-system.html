Why Track Outcomes?

TMS Patient Outcome Tracking System
Systematic outcome measurement is the cornerstone of high-quality TMS practice. Beyond clinical necessity, outcome data increasingly drives payer relations, regulatory compliance, and the advancement of the TMS evidence base.

Clinical Excellence

Identify non-responders at Week 4 and adjust stimulation parameters before the authorized course is complete. Early identification of partial response enables timely protocol modifications — changing coil placement, increasing intensity, or adjusting pulse frequency.

Insurance & Payer Relations

Aggregate outcome data supports continued treatment authorization and simplifies peer-to-peer reviews. A documented response trajectory gives medical directors clear clinical justification for ongoing care — reducing administrative friction and denials.

Referring Physician Marketing

Shareable outcome summaries (de-identified aggregate data) demonstrate practice quality to referring psychiatrists, primary care physicians, and pain specialists. This builds referral volume and strengthens professional network relationships.

Research & Publications

Consistent data collection across patients positions your practice to contribute to the TMS evidence base. Multi-site collaborations and journal submissions require standardized outcome measures — the data must be collected systematically to be usable.

Regulatory & FDA Post-Market Surveillance

FDA clearance for TMS devices carries post-market surveillance obligations. Documented adverse events and outcome measures support compliance reporting requirements and demonstrate ongoing device safety monitoring as part of your quality management system.

Core Outcome Measures

PHQ-9 Primary Measure Depression

Items: 9 Score range: 0 – 27 Clinically significant improvement: 5+ point decrease from baseline Remission threshold: Score < 5

Validated self-report tool for depression severity. Administered at baseline, Week 4, Week 6, post-treatment, and all follow-up points. Change score (baseline minus follow-up) is the primary efficacy endpoint for most TMS clinical protocols.

GAD-7 Anxiety

Items: 7 Score range: 0 – 21 Clinically significant improvement: 4+ point decrease from baseline Remission threshold: Score < 5

Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale. Often comorbid with depression in TMS patients. Capturing GAD-7 data alongside PHQ-9 provides a fuller clinical picture and supports treatment of anxiety-spectrum presentations.

CGI-S Clinician-Rated Baseline Only

Scale: 1 – 7 Baseline: Required at Week 0 (pre-treatment) Post-treatment: Required at post-treatment assessment Follow-up: Required at 1mo, 3mo, 6mo

Clinical Global Impression – Severity. Clinician-rated global assessment of illness severity. Score of 1 = normal, 4 = moderately ill, 7 = among the most extremely ill patients. Provides an independent anchor for self-reported PHQ-9 and GAD-7 scores.

CGI-I Clinician-Rated

Scale: 1 – 7 Timing: Week 2, Week 4, Week 6, post-treatment Interpretation: 1 = very much improved; 2 = much improved; 3 = minimally improved; 4 = no change; 5 = minimally worse; 6 = much worse; 7 = very much worse

Clinical Global Impression – Improvement. Clinician-rated global assessment of change from baseline. A CGI-I score of 1 or 2 (much or very much improved) combined with PHQ-9 improvement defines clinical response.

EQ-5D-5L

Dimensions: 5 Timing: Baseline and post-treatment

EuroQol quality-of-life instrument measuring mobility, self-care, usual activities, pain/discomfort, and anxiety/depression. Generates a health utility index used in health economics analyses and payer negotiations.

Patient Satisfaction Survey

Items: 10-item Likert scale Timing: Baseline (expectations) and post-treatment

Custom clinic-developed instrument capturing treatment experience, provider communication, scheduling convenience, perceived benefit, and likelihood to recommend. Compares pre- and post-treatment expectations vs. experience.

Response & Remission Definitions

TMS Patient Outcome Tracking System
Standardized outcome definitions enable meaningful comparison across patients, sites, and published literature. The following thresholds are consistent with FDA clinical trial endpoints and consensus guidelines from the Clinical TMS Society.
Category Definition PHQ-9 Criteria CGI-I Criteria Clinical Implication
No Response < 25% PHQ-9 improvement from baseline < 25% decrease CGI-I ≥ 4 Initiate Non-Response Protocol at Week 4. Consider protocol modification, switching to bilateral or right-sided DLPFC, or discontinuing TMS.
Partial Response 25 – 49% PHQ-9 improvement from baseline 25–49% decrease CGI-I 3 Continue current protocol. Assess at Week 6. Consider augmentation (e.g., pharmacotherapy adjustment). Document decision rationale.
Response ≥ 50% PHQ-9 improvement from baseline ≥ 50% decrease CGI-I ≤ 2 Continue full course. Plan taper and follow-up schedule. Discuss maintenance TMS if clinically indicated.
Remission PHQ-9 score < 5 at post-treatment Score < 5 CGI-I ≤ 2 (typical) Optimal outcome. Establish relapse prevention plan. 6-month follow-up schedule. Consider maintenance TMS.
Clinical Response Both CGI-I ≤ 2 AND ≥50% PHQ-9 improvement (strictest standard) ≥ 50% decrease CGI-I ≤ 2 Gold-standard endpoint for clinical trials and peer-to-peer payer reviews. Most defensible criterion for insurance authorization.

Adverse Event Tracking

All adverse events — regardless of perceived severity — must be documented in the Session Log and the patient record. Serious adverse events (SAEs) require immediate physician notification and FDA MedWatch reporting within 15 days.

Seizure

Involuntary convulsive activity during or within minutes of stimulation. Requires immediate protocol halt.

Always — SAE, reportable

Syncope

Loss of consciousness, with or without hypotension. May accompany seizure or occur independently.

Usually — SAE, reportable

Headache

Most common AE. Usually mild, transient, resolves with OTC analgesics. Escalation to moderate/severe requires intervention and documentation.

Mild–Moderate — intervention as needed

Pain at Stimulation Site

Scalp discomfort localized to coil position. Often relates to coil positioning, intensity above 110% MT, or pulse frequency.

Mild–Moderate — adjust parameters

Anxiety / Nervousness

Pre-session anticipatory anxiety; may also occur during stimulation. Usually self-limited. Persistent anxiety may indicate need for pretreatment anxiolytic.

Mild — reassess, monitor

Hearing Change / Tinnitus

Rare. Associated with acoustic artifact from coil discharge. Should not occur with proper earplug use (hearing protection mandatory).

Moderate — investigate hearing protection

Quality Improvement Workflow

TMS Patient Outcome Tracking System
A structured QI workflow ensures consistent clinical standards, enables early identification of protocol deviations, and creates a documented framework for continuous improvement. All QI activities should be reviewed at monthly staff meetings and summarized in quarterly aggregate reports.

Monthly Chart Audit Checklist

Per-Chart Review Items (audit 10% of active charts monthly)

□ Baseline PHQ-9, GAD-7, CGI-S, EQ-5D-5L all documented in chart

□ Week 4 PHQ-9 and CGI-I completed and reviewed

□ Non-response protocol initiated if <25% PHQ-9 improvement at Week 4

□ Adverse events logged in Session Log with severity classification

□ Authorization number and expiration date in patient record

□ Clinical status updated in Patient Roster tab

□ Follow-up appointments scheduled before treatment course completion

□ Informed consent for TMS updated within past 12 months

□ Motor threshold documented and re-verified if >10 days between sessions

□ Treatment intensity (% MT) within authorized range

□ Coil placement documented for each session

□ Post-treatment assessment completed within Week 7-9 window

Non-Response Protocol — Week 4 Decision Tree

Initiate when Week 4 PHQ-9 shows <25% improvement from baseline OR CGI-I ≥ 4.

1

Review Assessment Data Accuracy

Confirm PHQ-9 was completed correctly, patient understood items, and score was entered accurately. Rule out measurement error before changing treatment.

2

Review Treatment Delivery

Audit Session Log: verify motor threshold accuracy, coil placement, pulse count, intensity (% MT), and duration. Check for missed sessions or prolonged gaps.

3

Clinical Re-Evaluation

Prescriber reviews diagnosis, comorbidities, current medications, and psychosocial factors. Consider whether patient is taking TMS-compatible medications (no contraindications). Document evaluation in chart note.

4

Protocol Adjustment (choose one or combine)

A) Increase intensity: Raise to 125-130% MT if tolerated (max per device guidelines)
B) Increase pulses per session: Increase to 4,000-6,000 pulses if duration allows
C) Change target: Consider switching to right-sided DLPFC (for anxious patients) or bilateral stimulation
D) Extend course: Request additional authorized sessions from payer with clinical justification

5

Reassess at Week 6

Administer PHQ-9, GAD-7, and CGI-I at Week 6. Document trajectory. If still non-responsive, escalate to clinical director for case conference and consider discontinuation or referral for alternative treatment (ECT, ketamine, medication adjustment).

Protocol Adjustment Reference

Parameter Standard Value Adjustment Range Clinical Rationale
Stimulation Intensity 100–120% Motor Threshold Up to 130% MT (with justification) Higher intensity increases cortical activation; most evidence-based adjustment for partial responders
Pulses per Session 3,000 pulses (left DLPFC) Up to 6,000 pulses (accelerated protocols) Higher pulse count may increase efficacy; monitor for increased AEs (headache, pain)
Treatment Frequency 5 sessions/week (standard) 5 sessions/week (do not reduce); consider accelerated 2x/day with 45+ min gap Reducing below 5x/week reduces efficacy. Accelerated protocols are emerging evidence
Coil Placement Left DLPFC (5.5cm rule) Switch to right DLPFC (for anxious patients) or bilateral Right-sided DLPFC may better address anxiety; bilateral may improve efficacy in severe cases
Pulse Width Standard (4-6ms biphasic) iTBS protocol (3-pulse bursts at 50Hz) iTBS allows shorter session duration with comparable outcomes in some studies

Glossary of Terms

TMS Patient Outcome Tracking System
CGI-I — Clinical Global Impression – Improvement. A 7-point clinician-rated scale measuring change from baseline illness severity.
CGI-S — Clinical Global Impression – Severity. A 7-point clinician-rated scale measuring absolute illness severity at a single timepoint.
Clinical Significance — A change large enough to have practical importance for the patient or clinician, beyond statistical significance alone.
DLPFC — Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex. The primary TMS target for major depression; located above and lateral to the orbit.
DSM-5 — Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition. The standard diagnostic classification system used in the United States.
EQ-5D-5L — EuroQol 5-Dimension 5-Level questionnaire. A standardized quality-of-life instrument measuring mobility, self-care, activities, pain, and anxiety.
GAD-7 — Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale. A validated self-report tool for screening and measuring severity of anxiety.
iTBS — Intermittent Theta Burst Stimulation. An accelerated TMS protocol using 3-pulse bursts at 50Hz, delivered in 2-second trains with 8-second intervals.
MADRS — Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale. A 10-item clinician-rated scale for assessing depression severity; more sensitive to change than PHQ-9.
MDD — Major Depressive Disorder. The primary diagnosis for most TMS-treated patients; defined by DSM-5 criteria.
Motor Threshold (MT) — The minimum stimulus intensity required to produce a motor evoked potential (usually in the hand muscle). Used to calibrate TMS dosing.
NIMH — National Institute of Mental Health. The U.S. federal agency funding psychiatric research; its clinical trial methodology informs TMS outcome standards.
PHQ-9 — Patient Health Questionnaire-9. A 9-item validated self-report instrument for screening, diagnosing, and monitoring depression severity.
Psychometric — Relating to the measurement of psychological variables. Psychometric properties include reliability (consistency) and validity (accuracy).
Remission — The absence of clinically significant depressive symptoms; in this framework, defined as PHQ-9 < 5 post-treatment.
Response — Clinically meaningful improvement; in this framework, defined as ≥50% decrease in PHQ-9 from baseline AND CGI-I ≤ 2.
ROC Curve — Receiver Operating Characteristic curve. A plot of sensitivity against specificity used to evaluate the diagnostic accuracy of a measure at different thresholds.
SAE — Serious Adverse Event. Any adverse event that results in death, life-threatening injury, hospitalization, or persistent disability; requires FDA MedWatch reporting.
Sensitivity — The ability of a test to correctly identify those with a condition (true positive rate). High sensitivity minimizes false negatives.
Specificity — The ability of a test to correctly identify those without a condition (true negative rate). High specificity minimizes false positives.
Statistical Significance — A result unlikely to have occurred by chance alone, conventionally at p < 0.05. Does not necessarily imply clinical importance.
TMS Protocol — A defined set of TMS stimulation parameters (frequency, intensity, site, pulses, sessions) used to treat a specific condition.

Response Thresholds at a Glance

  • PHQ-9 clinical improvement: ≥5 point decrease
  • Response: ≥50% PHQ-9 decrease + CGI-I ≤2
  • Remission: PHQ-9 <5 at post-treatment
  • Non-response: <25% PHQ-9 improvement at Week 4
  • GAD-7 clinical improvement: ≥4 point decrease

Critical Decision Points

  • Week 2: First efficacy check — CGI-I only
  • Week 4: Non-response checkpoint — initiate QI protocol if <25% PHQ-9 improvement
  • Week 6: Post-adjustment reassessment
  • Post-Tx (Wk 7–9): Primary efficacy endpoint — all measures
  • At discharge: Schedule all 3 follow-up appointments before patient leaves
