1. Pre-Treatment Setup Protocol

Proper setup before the first patient of the day — and between patients — is critical for safety, treatment efficacy, and equipment longevity. Follow this protocol every morning before patient arrival and after every patient.

After each patient: (1) Change headrest cover or wipe with disinfectant. (2) Wipe coil with approved disinfectant wipe — do not submerge coil. (3) Change examination table paper if used. (4) Verify all treatment parameters are correct for next patient before calling them in.

3. Contraindication Screening Guide

Contraindication screening is the single most important safety step a TMS technician performs. A missed contraindication can result in serious harm. Never skip the screening — even for patients who have completed many sessions without incident.

Absolute Contraindications (TMS is NOT permitted)

Absolute Contraindications
  • Ferromagnetic implants within 30cm of coil — pacemakers, implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs), aneurysm clips, cochlear implants, vagus nerve stimulators (VNS), deep brain stimulators (DBS), electroencephalogram (EEG) electrodes
  • Active seizure disorder — patients with epilepsy who are not adequately controlled, or who have had a seizure within 6 months
  • Brain tumor or cerebral vascular lesion — in the treatment target area
  • Intracranial hemorrhage — active or recent
  • Severe head trauma — with skull fracture or intracranial injury in the past 12 months
Relative Contraindications
  • Pregnancy — safety not established; typically deferred unless risk-benefit justifies
  • History of seizures — but not active epilepsy; physician must approve and document risk
  • Substance abuse — active use may lower seizure threshold; clinical judgment required
  • Medications that lower seizure threshold — e.g., bupropion at high doses, clozapine; physician review needed
  • Headaches/migraines — may be exacerbated; may require pre-medication protocol

Patient Contraindication Screening Script

Before Every Session — Ask the Patient:

"Before we begin today, I need to ask you a few quick safety questions — the same ones we go through every session."

  1. "Since your last session, have you had any new medical procedures, surgeries, or emergency room visits?"
  2. "Have you had any new implants, devices, or metal placed in or on your body — especially in your head, neck, or chest?" (Probe: pacemakers, hearing aids, dental implants, IUDs, piercings)
  3. "Have you started any new medications since your last session?"
  4. "Have you experienced any seizures or episodes of loss of consciousness since your last session?"
  5. "Is there anything else you think we should know about your health before we start treatment today?"

Document all answers in the session log. Any "yes" answer requires supervisor review before proceeding.

4. Emergency Protocols

TMS is remarkably safe, but like all medical procedures, emergencies can occur. The most serious is a treatment-induced seizure, which occurs in approximately 0.1% of treatments in clinical practice. Your response in the first 60 seconds matters. Know these protocols by heart.

If a Seizure Occurs

1
STOP TREATMENT IMMEDIATELY
Press the emergency stop button on the device. Remove the coil from the patient's head. Do not attempt to restrain the patient.
2
CALL FOR HELP
Activate the emergency call button or call out for assistance. Have another staff member call 911 if you are alone with the patient.
3
PROTECT THE PATIENT
Clear the area around the treatment chair of any objects that could cause injury. Position the patient on their side (recovery position) once the active seizure phase has passed. Do not put anything in the patient's mouth.
4
MONITOR AND DOCUMENT
Time the seizure duration. Monitor breathing and pulse. Once the patient is stable, document: time seizure started, duration, description of movements, body parts involved, any post-ictal confusion. This information is required for the FDA MedWatch report.
5
NOTIFY THE PHYSICIAN AND ADMINISTRATION
Contact the treating physician immediately. Notify clinic administration. Complete incident report within 24 hours. Seizures must be reported to the FDA via MedWatch (1-800-FDA-1088 or online at FDA.gov/MedWatch).
SEIZURE WARNING SIGNS — Stop Treatment If You Observe:

Sudden facial twitching • Eye rolling upward • Rhythmic jerking movements of limbs • Loss of awareness • Inability to respond • Urinary incontinence • Tongue biting. Any of these = STOP TREATMENT and initiate seizure protocol immediately.

Syncope / Fainting Protocol

1
Stop treatment
Press emergency stop. Remove coil from head.
2
Position patient safely
If still in chair, recline chair fully. If on floor, do not move patient. Loosen any tight clothing.
3
Check responsiveness
Call patient's name. Check for pulse and breathing. If no pulse, begin CPR and call 911.
4
Recovery and documentation
If patient recovers quickly (typical for vasovagal syncope), allow 15–20 minutes of rest before sitting up. Offer water. Notify physician. Document episode.

Severe Headache Protocol

  • Stop treatment immediately and remove coil
  • Assess pain level (0–10 scale), location, quality (throbbing, sharp, dull)
  • If mild (1–4/10): allow patient to rest 5–10 minutes, offer water and quiet environment, resume treatment at reduced intensity if patient agrees and physician has approved
  • If moderate (5–7/10): do not resume today, offer acetaminophen if not contraindicated, schedule follow-up with treating physician before next session
  • If severe (8+/10) or sudden onset: do not resume, refer to physician or urgent care, consider neurological assessment, document thoroughly

Anxiety / Panic Attack Protocol

  • Stop treatment and remove coil
  • Speak calmly and reassuringly: "You're safe. This is a common reaction and will pass."
  • Guide patient to slow, deep breathing (4 counts in, 6 counts out)
  • Offer water, a quiet room, and time
  • Assess: Is this anxiety related to the TMS noise, positioning, or a panic disorder? Adjust protocol accordingly
  • If patient has a known panic disorder, discuss pre-session anxiety management with physician (e.g., 0.5mg lorazepam PRN 30 minutes before treatment)
  • Document episode and notify treating physician

5. Motor Threshold Mapping

The motor threshold (MT) is the minimum stimulation intensity required to produce an observable motor response (typically a twitch of the contralateral thumb). MT is the foundation of TMS dosing — all treatment intensities are expressed as a percentage of the individual's MT. Accurate MT determination is essential for both safety and efficacy.

Motor Threshold Determination Procedure

1
Prepare the patient
Explain the procedure: "I'm going to deliver single pulses to find the spot that makes your thumb twitch. You'll feel a tapping sensation on your scalp. Tell me if it's uncomfortable." Position patient with arm relaxed at side or resting on armrest. Ask patient to relax hand and fingers.
2
Locate the motor cortex
Place coil over the contralateral motor cortex — approximately 5cm anterior to the midpoint of the line connecting the tragus and the external auditory meatus (the "5cm rule"). Start on the right hemisphere (left DLPFC treatment = right motor cortex mapping).
3
Begin at starting intensity
Set device to single-pulse mode. Start at 40% maximum stimulator output (MSO). Deliver single pulses at 5-second intervals. Watch the patient's contralateral thumb for movement.
4
Titrate to threshold
Increase intensity in 5% steps until you observe a clear thumb twitch in at least 3 of 5 consecutive pulses. This is the Motor Threshold. Note the exact % MSO at which it occurs. Document the coil position using anatomical landmarks.
5
Set treatment intensity
Treatment intensity is set at 120% of the determined Motor Threshold. If MT = 45% MSO, treatment intensity = 54% MSO. Round to nearest whole number. Some protocols use 110% MT for anxious patients or those with lower thresholds.
MT Documentation Requirements

Record in the session log: (1) MT value in % MSO. (2) Coil position that produced MT (describe using anatomical landmarks). (3) Treatment intensity prescribed (% MT and absolute % MSO). (4) Whether MT was re-determined at any point during treatment course. MT should be re-determined if: the patient reports significantly different sensation, ≥10% change in device output, or at the physician's order.

Device-Specific Technician Notes

NeuroStar: Most widely deployed in US clinics. Coil must be held perpendicular to the scalp. Cleaning: wipe with 70% isopropyl alcohol wipe. Coil cable has a limited number of pulses before replacement — track in device log.

BrainsWay: Helmet system covers both hemispheres. Does NOT require single-motor threshold — uses a fixed dosing algorithm. Helmet requires cooling — check coolant daily. Cleaning: wipe helmet interior with approved disinfectant between patients.

MagVenture: Coil cooling is built into the coil housing — no external coolant needed. Cool-B70 coil allows higher pulse counts before heating. Software allows full protocol customization per physician order.

Nexstim: Uses MRI-guided neuronavigation for precise anatomical targeting. Requires additional training on the navigation system. Technician must be proficient in MRI safety protocols as well as TMS operation.

8. Patient Communication Scripts

How you communicate with patients before, during, and after treatment affects both their experience and their outcomes. Patients who feel informed and comfortable are more likely to complete the full course of treatment.

Before Treatment — What to Say

Session Introduction Script

"Hi [patient name]. Today's session will be session [number] of [total]. We'll be doing the same treatment as last time — about [X] minutes total. You'll feel a tapping sensation on the left side of your head, and you might notice your thumb twitching slightly during the mapping portion. If at any point you feel uncomfortable or have any concerns, just let me know and we can pause. Does that sound good? Do you have any questions before we start?"

Managing Patient Expectations — Key Points to Cover

  • TMS is not electric shock therapy — no anesthesia, no seizure induction, no pain medication needed
  • The tapping sensation is normal — it means the coil is working
  • The machine will make a loud clicking/rhythmic sound — earplugs are provided
  • Patients can read, watch a screen, or simply rest during treatment — eyes open or closed is their choice
  • There is no downtime — patients can drive and return to normal activities immediately after
  • Results build over time — most patients begin noticing improvement around session 10–15

During Treatment — What to Say

If Patient Asks "Is this normal?"

"Yes, absolutely. The tapping and any facial sensation you feel is completely normal — it means the coil is targeting the right area. Let me know if it ever feels painful."

If Patient Reports Headache

"I'm sorry you're feeling that. Headaches are one of the most common side effects, but they're usually mild. We'll finish this train and then I'll check in with you — we may be able to adjust the intensity slightly."

If Patient Seems Anxious

"You're doing great. Just relax — there's nothing you need to do. I'll be right here the whole time watching the machine. We'll be done before you know it."

If Patient Falls Asleep

No need to wake them unless coil position becomes unstable. Document: "Patient slept through remainder of treatment, well tolerated."

After Treatment — What to Say

Session Conclusion Script

"All done for today — [X] minutes, [X] pulses. How are you feeling? Any headache or discomfort? If you're doing okay, I'll have you schedule your next session before you go. Most people do best with five sessions a week, so we'd look at coming in [Mon/Wed/Fri] and [Tue/Thu] going forward. We'll see you then!"

Follow up with: "If you have any concerns before then — a headache that doesn't go away, any new symptoms, anything that worries you — here's our after-hours number: [PHONE NUMBER]."

Glossary of Key Terms

AED Automated External Defibrillator. Used in cardiac emergencies. TMS clinics should have one on-site and staff trained in its use.
Coil The electromagnetic coil that delivers magnetic pulses to the patient's scalp. Different coil geometries (figure-8, H-coil) target different brain regions and depths.
DTF Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex. The brain region targeted by standard TMS for depression treatment. Located on the lateral surface of the frontal lobe.
Excitatory Threshold The stimulation intensity required to increase neural activity in the target region. Different from the motor threshold.
FDA Cleared FDA designation meaning a device has been reviewed and found substantially equivalent to a predicate device. NeuroStar, BrainsWay, MagVenture, Nexstim.
Frequency The number of magnetic pulses delivered per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). Standard TMS uses 10Hz (high-frequency, excitatory) or 1Hz (low-frequency, inhibitory).
Hertz Hz. Unit of frequency. 10Hz = 10 pulses per second. Used to describe TMS pulse frequency.
Inhibitory Threshold The stimulation intensity at which TMS produces neural inhibition rather than excitation. Used in low-frequency (1Hz) protocols for anxiety or right-sided treatment.
ITI Inter-Train Interval. The rest period between consecutive TMS pulse trains. Standard: 26 seconds at 10Hz.
Motor Threshold MT. The minimum stimulation intensity (expressed as % MSO) required to produce an observable motor response (thumb twitch) when applied to the motor cortex.
MT Abbreviation for Motor Threshold. Used as the basis for all TMS dosing (e.g., 120% MT).
Neuroplasticity The brain's ability to form new neural connections. TMS is believed to work by inducing neuroplastic changes in the prefrontal cortex, normalizing dysregulated neural circuits in depression.
Pulse Width The duration of each individual magnetic pulse, measured in microseconds (μs). Broader pulse widths deliver more energy per pulse. Typical: 120–180 μs.
rTMS Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation. The standard form of TMS treatment involving repeated pulses over a session. Distinct from single-pulse TMS used in research/assessment.
Session A single TMS treatment appointment. A standard course = 36 sessions over 6–9 weeks (5 sessions/week). Each session = 18–37 minutes of active stimulation.
Theta Burst iTBS / cTBS. A TMS protocol using clustered pulses (typically 3 pulses at 50Hz) delivered in a theta rhythm (5Hz pattern). FDA-cleared alternative to standard 10Hz protocols. Shorter sessions (3 min 9 sec vs. 19 min).
Train A continuous sequence of TMS pulses delivered without interruption. Standard TMS: 4-second train of 10Hz pulses = 40 pulses per train, followed by a 26-second rest (ITI).
Treatment Intensity The strength of each magnetic pulse, expressed as a percentage of the patient's motor threshold (e.g., 120% MT). Higher intensity = stronger magnetic field = greater neural activation.
